## Clinical Arc

Elena grew up in San Antonio and moved to Austin in her mid-twenties to follow a career in UX design. She was healthy through most of her twenties — the usual stuff, a sprained wrist from a mountain bike fall, seasonal allergies she managed with loratadine, a handful of urgent care visits for strep throat. Her PCP in Austin, Dr. Patricia Nguyen at Central Austin Family Medicine, had been seeing her since 2017 for annual wellness visits and was generally a reassuring constant in her life.

**The family-building years (2018–2021)**

When Elena and Marco decided to start a family in 2018, she established care with Lone Star Women's Health, an OB/GYN group practice near downtown Austin. Her first pregnancy, conceived in early 2019, was straightforward. She had the standard first-trimester labs and anatomy scan, gained appropriate weight, had mild round-ligament discomfort in the second trimester, and delivered vaginally at 39+2 weeks in November 2019. Her son, Daniel, was healthy at birth.

The second pregnancy, which began in December 2019, ended badly. By ten weeks, in early February 2020, Elena had an ultrasound showing no fetal cardiac activity — a missed miscarriage at approximately 9 weeks and 4 days of development. Her OB, Dr. Carmen Villanueva, counseled expectant management given the early gestational age and stable clinical picture. There was no hemorrhage, no infection, no surgical intervention. The tissue passed over about two weeks. Physically, Elena recovered without complication. Emotionally, it was hard in ways that took longer to process. It is documented in her OB chart as a spontaneous abortion, which is the correct clinical term, and the encounter notes reflect the emotional support counseling Dr. Villanueva provided.

Elena became pregnant again in mid-2021. This third pregnancy was uneventful until about 28 weeks, when a routine glucose challenge test came back elevated. A three-hour glucose tolerance test confirmed gestational diabetes mellitus — values not severe enough to require insulin, but clearly abnormal. Dr. Villanueva referred Elena to the practice's registered dietitian, and Elena adapted well, managing her blood glucose entirely through carbohydrate counting and walking after meals. Her A1c at delivery was 5.4%. She delivered her daughter, Sofia, vaginally at 39+5 weeks in April 2022. Postpartum glucose normalized immediately; repeat fasting glucose at her six-week postpartum visit was 88 mg/dL. Dr. Villanueva noted the gestational diabetes history and recommended annual A1c screening going forward, given the elevated lifetime risk of type 2 diabetes.

Through the family-building period, Elena continued seeing Dr. Nguyen at Central Austin Family Medicine for non-OB matters. She got her influenza vaccines there each fall, a Tdap booster in 2018, and had a couple of telehealth visits during COVID for an upper respiratory illness in the winter of 2020 and a sinus infection in the spring of 2021. Her blood pressure, lipids, and thyroid panel were all within normal limits. She was not on any regular medications beyond the prenatal vitamins that bookended her pregnancies and the loratadine she used seasonally.

**The move to California and the emergence of something new (2022–2023)**

Marco's company relocated their engineering team to the Bay Area, and Elena, now with a two-year-old and a newborn, made the practical decision to follow. They settled in the Temescal neighborhood of Oakland in October 2022. The chaos of the move — new city, new daycare arrangements, breastfeeding Sofia, working freelance — made the first few months a blur.

In early 2023, something started that Elena initially attributed to carrying a toddler too much. Both of her hands ached. Her wrists felt stiff in the morning, stiff enough that she had trouble opening jars before she'd been up for an hour. She chalked it up to the physical demands of caring for two small children, but by March the stiffness was lasting more than 90 minutes into the day, and she noticed her knuckles looked subtly swollen in the mornings. She'd established care with a new PCP, Dr. Anita Okonkwo at Eastbay Primary Care Associates in Oakland, in November 2022 — a wellness visit to re-establish care and update her immunizations (COVID booster, flu shot). She called Dr. Okonkwo's office in March 2023.

Dr. Okonkwo examined Elena and noted bilateral metacarpophalangeal and proximal interphalangeal joint tenderness with mild soft-tissue swelling, worse on the right. She ordered a rheumatologic workup: rheumatoid factor came back 84 IU/mL (reference <14), anti-cyclic citrullinated peptide antibody at 112 units (strongly positive), ESR at 52 mm/hr, CRP at 18 mg/L. The picture was clear. Dr. Okonkwo referred Elena to Bay Area Rheumatology Associates in Oakland, and Elena was seen by Dr. James Tran within three weeks.

Dr. Tran confirmed the diagnosis: seropositive rheumatoid arthritis, likely at least several months into its course given the serology levels. He discussed the treatment landscape with Elena — the importance of early aggressive treatment to prevent irreversible joint damage, the role of disease-modifying antirheumatic drugs, the specifics of methotrexate. Elena was given time to ask questions and went home to read for a week before starting. She began methotrexate 15 mg orally once weekly in April 2023, along with folic acid 1 mg daily to reduce side effects.

Monitoring became a new rhythm in Elena's life. Every 8–12 weeks, labs: complete blood count, liver function tests (AST, ALT, albumin), creatinine. She tolerated methotrexate reasonably well — some nausea on injection days, which she managed by taking it Friday evenings so the worst was over before the workweek. But by the fall of 2023, despite a modest reduction in morning stiffness duration (down from 90+ minutes to about 45–60 minutes), her disease activity score remained moderately elevated. Several joints were still persistently tender and swollen. Dr. Tran and Elena agreed it was time to escalate.

In November 2023, adalimumab (Humira) 40 mg subcutaneous injection every two weeks was added to her methotrexate. Elena was counseled on infection risk, instructed on self-injection technique, and cleared (negative QuantiFERON-TB, normal chest X-ray interpreted at Bay Area Imaging but ordered through rheumatology). She also received her pneumococcal vaccine (PCV15) and a zoster vaccine series around this time, per guidelines for patients starting biologic therapy.

**The payoff (2024–present)**

By mid-2024, the combination was working. Elena's morning stiffness had dropped to under 30 minutes, and some mornings she barely noticed it. The metacarpophalangeal swelling was resolving. Her CRP had come down to 4 mg/L, ESR to 22 mm/hr, anti-CCP titers were declining slowly. Dr. Tran moved her to quarterly visits, which felt like a milestone.

In August 2024, Elena was playing with Daniel and Sofia at Joaquin Miller Park when she stepped awkwardly on a tree root and badly inverted her right ankle. She drove herself to Bay Area Urgent Care on Telegraph Avenue that evening, where X-rays ruled out fracture and she was diagnosed with a Grade II lateral ankle sprain — anterior talofibular ligament involvement. She was given an air stirrup brace and ibuprofen (with Dr. Tran's concurrent RA management in mind — she flagged the biologic use to the urgent care provider, who noted it in the chart). She followed up with Dr. Okonkwo's office by telehealth three days later to confirm the ankle was improving as expected.

Through 2025 and into 2026, Elena has continued quarterly rheumatology visits with Dr. Tran and annual wellness visits with Dr. Okonkwo. Her most recent labs (February 2026) show methotrexate and adalimumab holding her disease in low-activity range. She has resumed regular yoga and has begun discussing with Dr. Tran the possibility of eventually tapering adalimumab if she sustains remission for another 12 months. The gestational diabetes history has prompted annual A1c checks; all have been normal to date (most recent 5.2%). She breastfed Sofia until about 14 months; her reproductive plans at present are open but not actively pursued.

## What This Patient Exercises for the Demo

Elena Reyes is designed to make the abstract mechanics of SMART Permission Tickets **viscerally concrete**. Here is what each constraint dimension demonstrates through her record:

### Jurisdiction Filtering (TX vs. CA)

Elena has records in two states with a hard geographic break at her October 2022 move. Every encounter at Central Austin Family Medicine (NPI 1437826095) and Lone Star Women's Health (NPI 1589043712) is Texas-jurisdiction data. Every encounter at Eastbay Primary Care Associates (NPI 1902847536), Bay Area Rheumatology Associates (NPI 1673910284), and Bay Area Urgent Care (NPI 1758294031) is California-jurisdiction data.

A permission ticket scoped to California jurisdiction returns her entire RA management story without touching a single obstetric encounter. The sensitivity separation is clean and real — not artificial. It demonstrates that jurisdiction filtering is not merely a geographic abstraction but can correspond directly to a patient's lived privacy concern.

### Scope Filtering (Clinical Resource Types)

A ticket requesting `patient/Condition.rs + patient/Observation.rs + patient/MedicationRequest.rs` would return Elena's RA diagnosis, her disease activity monitoring labs, and her current DMARD/biologic medications — everything a new rheumatologist needs — without pulling DocumentReference (which would include the miscarriage counseling note), Procedure (which includes delivery codes), or Encounter resources that would expose the pregnancy narrative. The reproductive history exists as valid FHIR; it is simply excluded by the scope constraint.

Conversely, a ticket scoped to `patient/DocumentReference.rs` would pull progress notes from all sites, potentially including the emotionally sensitive miscarriage counseling note from Dr. Villanueva's encounter in February 2020 — demonstrating that scope selection is not consequence-free and patients benefit from understanding what each scope exposes.

### Period Filtering (Date Range)

A ticket with a period constraint of `2023-01-01` to present excludes the entire Texas chapter. The miscarriage (February 2020), both Texas deliveries (November 2019, April 2022), and the gestational diabetes management (2021–2022) all fall before this window. The period filter is a blunt but highly effective privacy tool for Elena: since she relocated in October 2022 and her new medical life began in November 2022, even a period starting at `2022-11-01` cleanly separates old from new.

Period filtering also demonstrates that the filter acts on **encounter date**, not record creation date — a re-entered historical allergy (amoxicillin, originally identified in childhood) might appear in all records regardless of period, and the system behavior around this edge case is worth exercising.

### Organization Filtering (Site-Level Access Control)

Four distinct organizations across two states offer fine-grained organization filtering scenarios:

- **Share only Rheumatology**: A ticket constrained to NPI 1673910284 (Bay Area Rheumatology Associates) gives a requesting clinician precisely the RA management data — no PCP notes, no urgent care records, no Texas history.
- **Share PCP + Rheumatology**: Combining NPIs 1902847536 and 1673910284 adds the initial diagnostic workup context that Dr. Okonkwo generated, useful for a consultant who wants to understand how the RA was identified.
- **Exclude Lone Star Women's Health**: A ticket that explicitly excludes NPI 1589043712 (or equivalently, does not include it in a positive allowlist) ensures the OB records are never part of any exchange, regardless of other scope or period settings.
- **Cross-state emergency scenario**: A hypothetical emergency in which a provider needs the complete medication list — methotrexate, adalimumab — from the Texas PCP and California records could be scoped to include only medication-related resources across all organizations, demonstrating that organization and scope constraints compose.

### The Core Demonstration Point

Elena's record is compelling because the filtering is not hypothetical. The data that gets filtered out — the miscarriage, the gestational diabetes, the delivery codes — is **real FHIR data sitting in the synthetic EHR**. When a demo shows that a permission ticket with California jurisdiction + 2023-present period + RA-relevant scopes returns exactly the rheumatology story and nothing else, observers can immediately grasp both what is shared and what is protected. The protection is meaningful because the protected data is sensitive in ways that current law — in Texas specifically — makes acutely consequential.

This patient also demonstrates that privacy decisions are not one-size-fits-all even within a single patient's record. Elena might willingly share her Texas PCP data (routine wellness, immunizations) but not her OB data, even though both are Texas records. The organization filter lets her make that distinction. The combination of period + jurisdiction + organization + scope gives her genuine control, not just the appearance of it.
