SUBJECTIVE:

Chief Complaint: Bilateral hand and wrist pain and morning stiffness

History of Present Illness:
Elena is a 36-year-old female presenting with a 3-4 month history of progressive bilateral hand and wrist pain and morning stiffness. Symptoms began insidiously in November or December 2022. She initially attributed the discomfort to repetitive strain from caring for her two young children (ages 3 years and 11 months). However, symptoms have worsened over the past several weeks despite activity modification and over-the-counter ibuprofen use.

Morning stiffness has become increasingly prominent and now lasts greater than 90 minutes each day—substantially longer than can be attributed to inactivity alone. Elena notes that ibuprofen provides only partial relief. Within the past 2-3 weeks, she has noticed visible mild swelling at the knuckles of both hands, particularly the right hand, which prompted her to call for an appointment.

She denies fever, night sweats, rash, psoriatic lesions, or oral ulcers. She denies Raynaud's phenomenon or dry eyes/mouth (sicca symptoms). She has not had prior episodes of polyarthritis. No known family history of rheumatoid arthritis or other autoimmune disease. No recent illness or travel. She reports feeling stressed about her health but otherwise denies depression or anxiety symptoms.

Medications: Loratadine 10 mg daily PRN for seasonal allergies. No other medications.

Allergies: Amoxicillin (penicillin class) — rash/hives in childhood; documented in chart.

ASSESSMENT:

Acute Presentation Consistent with Early Inflammatory Arthritis, Likely Rheumatoid Arthritis

Clinical reasoning: Patient presents with insidious onset symmetric polyarthritis predominantly involving the small joints of the hands (MCP and PIP joints) with DIP sparing. Prominent morning stiffness lasting >90 minutes is a classic hallmark of inflammatory rather than mechanical arthritis. Age 36, female sex, and ethnic background (Hispanic/Latina) align with epidemiology of RA. Physical exam confirms soft tissue swelling and tenderness at involved joints. Partial response to ibuprofen is consistent with inflammatory etiology.
