## Clinical Arc

Kevin's health history is relatively unremarkable until his late forties. He established care at Minnehaha Internal Medicine in 2018 after his previous PCP retired. At that initial visit, his blood pressure was 148/92 mmHg on repeat measurement, and Dr. Angela Whitfield diagnosed stage 1 hypertension. They agreed to a trial of lifestyle modification — reduced sodium, increased exercise. When a follow-up three months later showed persistent elevation (142/88), Dr. Whitfield started lisinopril 10 mg daily. Kevin responded well; subsequent readings have generally been in the 126–134/78–84 range.

At his 2019 annual wellness exam, fasting lipids showed total cholesterol of 218 mg/dL, LDL 138 mg/dL, HDL 52 mg/dL, and triglycerides 140 mg/dL. Dr. Whitfield discussed statin therapy, but Kevin preferred to manage with diet and exercise. He cut back on red meat, increased fish intake, and added a daily 30-minute walk. By his 2020 annual exam, LDL had come down to 124 mg/dL — still borderline, but trending in the right direction. They agreed to continue monitoring. His lipid panels in 2021 through 2024 have remained in the borderline range (LDL 118–132), not quite reaching the threshold where both patient and provider felt a statin was necessary.

His other preventive care has been routine. He received a screening colonoscopy in 2019 (normal, repeat in 10 years). He had a small seborrheic keratosis removed from his upper back in 2021 — benign on pathology. He received his COVID-19 primary series and boosters on schedule. Flu shots annually. He had a mild bout of acute sinusitis in early 2023, treated with a short course of amoxicillin. No known drug allergies, though he reports mild GI upset with ibuprofen (listed as intolerance, not allergy). He takes no other medications beyond lisinopril 10 mg daily and an over-the-counter fish oil supplement.

**The atrial fibrillation arc begins in late 2025.** In November 2025, Kevin notices intermittent palpitations — a fluttering sensation in his chest, mostly while playing golf or walking uphill. He also notices he gets winded more easily than usual on exertion, needing to pause on the back nine where he normally wouldn't. He chalks it up to the cold weather and getting older. He mentions it almost in passing to Soon-Yi, who urges him to bring it up at his next doctor's visit.

At a routine hypertension follow-up on **January 12, 2026**, Kevin mentions the palpitations to Dr. Whitfield. On examination, she discovers an irregularly irregular pulse at a rate of 94 bpm. Blood pressure is 138/86. She performs an in-office **12-lead ECG**, which confirms **atrial fibrillation** with a ventricular rate of 92 bpm, no ST-segment changes, normal axis. She discusses the finding with Kevin, explaining that this is a common heart rhythm disorder but one that needs evaluation and management.

Dr. Whitfield orders a workup: **transthoracic echocardiogram (TTE)**, **TSH**, **CBC**, and **comprehensive metabolic panel**. Labs are drawn that day. TSH comes back normal at 2.1 mIU/L. CBC is unremarkable (hemoglobin 14.8, WBC 6.2, platelets 234). CMP is normal (creatinine 0.94, potassium 4.1, glucose 98). The echocardiogram is performed on **January 19, 2026** at an affiliated imaging center and shows **mild left atrial dilation (4.3 cm)**, **preserved left ventricular ejection fraction (58%)**, no significant valvular disease, and no pericardial effusion. Estimated right ventricular systolic pressure is normal.

At a follow-up visit on **January 26, 2026**, Dr. Whitfield reviews the results with Kevin. She calculates his **CHA₂DS₂-VASc score at 1** (1 point for hypertension; he is male, under 65, no diabetes, no prior stroke/TIA, no vascular disease, no heart failure). Given the score of 1, anticoagulation is reasonable but involves shared decision-making. She discusses stroke risk and bleeding risk. Kevin, understanding the stakes, agrees to start anticoagulation. She prescribes:

- **Metoprolol succinate 50 mg daily** for rate control
- **Apixaban 5 mg twice daily** for anticoagulation
- Continues **lisinopril 10 mg daily**

She also discusses the option of catheter ablation as a potential rhythm-control strategy, particularly given Kevin's relatively young age, active lifestyle, and symptomatic episodes. She refers Kevin to the **University of Minnesota Cardiac Electrophysiology Clinic** for evaluation. A referral note and **ServiceRequest** are generated, citing the clinical question: *"57-year-old male with new-onset atrial fibrillation, mild LA dilation, preserved EF. Please evaluate for catheter ablation candidacy."*

Kevin's **initial consultation with Dr. James Lindgren**, a cardiac electrophysiologist at the University of Minnesota, occurs on **February 16, 2026**. Dr. Lindgren reviews the ECG, echocardiogram report, labs, and medication list obtained from Minnehaha Internal Medicine via a Permission Ticket. He performs his own examination, confirming AF with controlled rate (ventricular rate 68 on metoprolol). He orders a **48-hour Holter monitor** to assess AF burden and rate control adequacy.

Kevin wears the Holter monitor from **February 18–20, 2026**. Results show atrial fibrillation throughout (persistent rather than paroxysmal), average ventricular rate of 72 bpm, no pauses greater than 2 seconds, no ventricular ectopy of concern.

At a follow-up on **March 9, 2026**, Dr. Lindgren reviews the Holter results with Kevin and Soon-Yi. He explains that Kevin has **persistent atrial fibrillation** and is a reasonable candidate for **pulmonary vein isolation (catheter ablation)**. They discuss success rates (approximately 70–80% for persistent AF with possible need for repeat procedure), risks (including rare complications like cardiac tamponade, pulmonary vein stenosis, phrenic nerve injury), and alternatives (continued rate control alone, antiarrhythmic drugs). Kevin expresses interest in ablation given his active lifestyle and ongoing symptoms despite rate control. They tentatively plan the procedure and Kevin is scheduled for a pre-procedure visit.

## Demo Relevance

Kevin Park's record is designed to exercise the **UC7 provider-to-provider consult** workflow for the SMART Permission Tickets reference implementation. Here is how his data maps to the key access constraint dimensions:

### Scopes (Resource-Type and Clinical Relevance Filtering)

The EP specialist (Dr. Lindgren) needs a focused subset of Kevin's PCP records — not everything. The Permission Ticket should demonstrate the ability to request:

- **Relevant resources:** ECG (DiagnosticReport + DocumentReference), echocardiogram report (DiagnosticReport + DocumentReference), labs (TSH, CBC, CMP as Observations/DiagnosticReports), medication list (MedicationRequests for lisinopril, metoprolol, apixaban), active conditions (atrial fibrillation, hypertension), and the referral ServiceRequest.
- **Excludable resources:** Kevin's colonoscopy from 2019, the seborrheic keratosis removal, his sinusitis episode, dermatology-adjacent records, PHQ-2 depression screenings, and immunization history are all clinically irrelevant to the EP consult. A well-scoped Permission Ticket should be able to exclude these, demonstrating that not all data flows to the specialist.

This tests whether scope constraints can distinguish cardiac-relevant data from the broader longitudinal record.

### Organizations (Community PCP → Academic Specialist)

The data flow is from **Minnehaha Internal Medicine** (NPI: 1234567890, a small community internal medicine practice) to the **University of Minnesota Cardiac Electrophysiology Clinic** (NPI: 1987654321, an academic medical center specialty clinic). This is a classic referral pattern — a community PCP referring to an academic specialist — and tests:

- The requesting organization is different from the data-holding organization.
- The Permission Ticket must identify the requester (Dr. Lindgren, NPI: 1345678902, at U of M) and the data holder (Minnehaha IM).
- Trust establishment between a small practice and a large academic system.

### Periods (Temporal Scoping)

While the EP specialist could reasonably request Kevin's full history, the most relevant data is from **January 2026 onward** (when the AFib was discovered). A Permission Ticket could demonstrate temporal constraints by:

- Requesting records from 2026-01-01 forward (capturing the AFib workup).
- Or requesting a broader window (e.g., 2023-01-01 forward) to include baseline wellness data for comparison.
- Kevin's records before 2023 are minimally relevant to the consult and could be excluded by period constraints.

### Jurisdictions (Single-State Simplicity)

Both sites are in **Minnesota**, making this a single-jurisdiction scenario. This is intentionally simple — it demonstrates that the Permission Ticket framework works cleanly within one state's regulatory environment without cross-state complexity. It serves as a baseline case against which multi-jurisdiction scenarios can be compared.

### Subject Resolution

Kevin is an **existing patient at Minnehaha Internal Medicine**, so the Permission Ticket references him by his patient resource reference at that site. The EP specialist's system resolves Kevin's identity through the referral linkage — the ServiceRequest from Dr. Whitfield includes Kevin's patient reference, and the Permission Ticket carries this forward. This tests the "subject resolution by reference" pattern rather than demographic matching.

### Requester Identity

The requester is **Dr. James Lindgren** (NPI: 1345678902), a licensed electrophysiologist at the University of Minnesota. The Permission Ticket includes his Practitioner reference and NPI, along with the reason for the request (atrial fibrillation, SNOMED code 49436004) and a reference to the originating ServiceRequest. This demonstrates practitioner-level (not patient-level) requesting, which is the defining characteristic of UC7.
