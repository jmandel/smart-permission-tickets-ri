SHARED DECISION-MAKING DISCUSSION:
Extensive discussion conducted with Mr. Park and his wife regarding catheter ablation for persistent atrial fibrillation. The following topics were addressed in detail:

Procedure: Pulmonary vein isolation via catheter ablation, performed under general anesthesia, typically 2-3 hours in duration. Transseptal access to the left atrium with radiofrequency or cryoablation energy to achieve electrical isolation of the pulmonary veins.

Success rates: Approximately 70-80% chance of freedom from AF at 1 year with a single procedure for persistent AF. Approximately 10-20% of patients require a second (redo) procedure.

Risks: Cardiac tamponade (<1%), pulmonary vein stenosis (<1%), phrenic nerve injury (<1%), vascular access complications (1-2%), stroke/TIA (<0.5%), esophageal injury (rare). Overall serious complication rate approximately 2-4%. Risks of general anesthesia also reviewed.

Recovery: Typically one overnight hospital stay. Activity restriction for 1-2 weeks. Anticoagulation continues for minimum 3 months post-ablation. Antiarrhythmic medication may be used during the 3-month blanking period. Expected return to full golf activity within 4-6 weeks post-procedure.

Alternatives discussed: Continue rate control alone with metoprolol; trial of antiarrhythmic drug therapy (flecainide, amiodarone) as alternatives to ablation.

Patient and wife asked appropriate questions regarding impact on daily activities and anesthesia risks. After thorough discussion, Mr. Park expressed strong preference to proceed with catheter ablation given his ongoing exertional symptoms and desire to maintain his active lifestyle. Decision to proceed was made collaboratively.
