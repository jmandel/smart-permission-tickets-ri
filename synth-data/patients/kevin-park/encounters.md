Rate control on metoprolol is adequate at rest but Kevin remains symptomatic, particularly with exertion. He is a reasonable candidate for **pulmonary vein isolation (PVI) via catheter ablation**.

Dr. Lindgren provides a thorough discussion of catheter ablation:

- **Success rates:** Approximately 70–80% chance of AF freedom at 1 year for persistent AF with a single procedure; approximately 10–20% of patients require a second (redo) procedure.
- **Risks:** Small risk of cardiac tamponade (<1%), pulmonary vein stenosis (<1%), phrenic nerve injury (<1%), vascular access complications (1–2%), stroke/TIA (<0.5%), esophageal injury (rare). Overall serious complication rate approximately 2–4%.
- **Procedure:** Performed under general anesthesia, typically 2–3 hours, with transseptal access to the left atrium. Radiofrequency or cryoablation energy used to create electrical isolation of the pulmonary veins.
- **Recovery:** Usually one night in hospital. 1–2 weeks of activity restriction. Anticoagulation continues for at least 3 months post-ablation. Antiarrhythmic medication may be used during the 3-month "blanking period."
- **Alternatives:** Continue rate control alone (metoprolol), trial of antiarrhythmic drug therapy (e.g., flecainide, amiodarone) as alternatives to ablation.

Kevin asks several questions about the impact on his golf game and daily routine. Dr. Lindgren estimates he could return to full golf activity within 4–6 weeks post-procedure. Soon-Yi asks about the anesthesia risks; these are reviewed as well.

Kevin expresses strong interest in pursuing ablation given his ongoing symptoms and desire to maintain his active lifestyle. He and Dr. Lindgren agree to proceed with scheduling the procedure.
