## Clinical Arc

He went through his twenties and thirties with nothing more than the occasional cold, an annual flu shot at the pharmacy, and rare visits to his primary care physician for a work physical.

In 2021, at age 42, James strained his right rotator cuff lifting heavy framing lumber overhead at a job site. He saw Dr. Laura Petersen at Heartland Surgical Associates in Omaha for an orthopedic evaluation. An MRI showed a partial-thickness supraspinatus tear without full rupture. The rotator cuff issue resolved fully, and he was discharged from orthopedic follow-up by mid-2021. His only standing medication from that era was occasional ibuprofen PRN for shoulder soreness, which he stopped needing by late 2021.

James had no primary care physician on record since about 2019, when he had a routine physical at a walk-in clinic. He was not actively followed by anyone for chronic disease — he simply did not have any.

### The Acute Surgical Episode (January–February 2026)

On the morning of January 12, 2026, James was at a commercial construction site in west Omaha when he developed sudden-onset crampy pain in his periumbilical region. Over the next several hours the pain migrated to his right lower quadrant and intensified. By mid-afternoon he was unable to work. A coworker drove him to the Emergency Department at Nebraska Medicine, the academic medical center affiliated with the University of Nebraska Medical Center in Omaha.

**ED Presentation (January 12, 2026):** James arrived at the Nebraska Medicine ED at approximately 15:40. His vitals on arrival were: temperature 100.9°F (38.3°C), heart rate 102, blood pressure 142/88, respiratory rate 18, SpO2 98% on room air. He reported 7/10 right lower quadrant pain, worse with movement, with associated nausea but no vomiting. Physical exam revealed RLQ tenderness with guarding and rebound. A CBC showed WBC 16,800/μL with 82% neutrophils, hemoglobin 14.2 g/dL, platelets 238,000. BMP was unremarkable (creatinine 1.0, glucose 118). CRP was 14.8 mg/dL. A CT abdomen/pelvis with IV contrast revealed a dilated, non-filling appendix with periappendiceal fat stranding, a small extraluminal gas collection, and a 2.3 cm rim-enhancing fluid collection adjacent to the cecum — consistent with perforated appendicitis with a localized abscess.

The surgical team was consulted. Dr. Marcus Okonkwo, an acute care surgeon at Nebraska Medicine, evaluated James and recommended emergent laparoscopic appendectomy.

**Emergent Appendectomy (January 12, 2026, evening):** James was taken to the operating room at 19:15. Under general anesthesia, Dr. Okonkwo performed a laparoscopic appendectomy with drainage of the periappendiceal abscess. The operative report describes a gangrenous appendix with a visible perforation at the tip and approximately 30 mL of purulent fluid in the right paracolic gutter. The abscess cavity was irrigated and a 10-French Jackson-Pratt drain was placed in the right lower quadrant. Estimated blood loss was 50 mL. The specimen was sent to pathology. The procedure lasted 68 minutes.

**Pathology Report (resulted January 14, 2026):** The surgical pathology report (accession SP-2026-00412) confirmed acute gangrenous appendicitis with transmural necrosis and perforation. There was acute serositis with fibrinopurulent exudate. No neoplasm was identified. Margins were uninvolved.

**Inpatient Stay — Initial Hospitalization (January 12–15, 2026):** James was admitted to the surgical floor post-operatively. He received IV piperacillin-tazobactam 3.375 g every 6 hours for perioperative infection coverage. His post-operative course was initially uncomplicated. He was afebrile by post-op day 1. His JP drain output decreased from 45 mL on POD1 to 15 mL on POD2, and the drain was removed on POD2. He tolerated a regular diet by POD1 and ambulated independently. His WBC trended down to 11,200 on POD2.

He was discharged on January 15, 2026 (hospital day 3 / POD3) in stable condition. Discharge medications included amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg twice daily for 7 days, acetaminophen 500 mg every 6 hours PRN pain, and oxycodone 5 mg every 4–6 hours PRN for breakthrough pain (dispensed #20). Discharge instructions included wound care for port sites, drain site monitoring, and return precautions for fever, worsening pain, or wound changes. A follow-up appointment was scheduled with Dr. Okonkwo's outpatient clinic at Heartland Surgical Associates for January 26, 2026 (post-op day 14).

**Allergies:** James has no known drug allergies. This was confirmed at ED triage and documented through his hospitalization.

### Surgical Site Infection and Readmission

**Surgeon Follow-Up — SSI Detected (January 22, 2026):** Four days before his scheduled follow-up, James called Heartland Surgical Associates reporting increasing redness and drainage from his right-sided port site. He was seen the same day by Dr. Okonkwo in the office. Examination revealed a 3 cm area of erythema and induration surrounding the 12 mm port incision in the right lower quadrant, with purulent drainage expressed on palpation. James reported subjective fevers at home. In-office vitals showed temperature 101.8°F (38.8°C), heart rate 94, BP 138/82. Given the fever, purulence, and concern for possible deeper surgical site or intra-abdominal infection, Dr. Okonkwo directed James to present to the Nebraska Medicine ED for readmission.

**Readmission (January 22–26, 2026):** James was admitted through the Nebraska Medicine ED on the evening of January 22 with a diagnosis of post-operative surgical site infection following laparoscopic appendectomy. Admission labs showed WBC 14,600 with left shift, CRP 11.2 mg/dL. Blood cultures were drawn (ultimately no growth at 5 days). A wound culture from the purulent port site drainage grew Escherichia coli (pan-sensitive) and mixed anaerobes.

A CT abdomen/pelvis with contrast was performed on January 23 to evaluate for intra-abdominal abscess. It showed a small 1.8 cm residual fluid collection in the right lower quadrant near the prior appendiceal bed, decreased from the original 2.3 cm collection. There was no drainable abscess, and interventional radiology was not consulted.

James was started on IV piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 g every 8 hours. His wound was opened, irrigated, and packed with wet-to-dry dressing changes twice daily. By hospital day 2 he was afebrile. By hospital day 3, WBC normalized to 9,800 and CRP was trending down to 4.1. The wound was granulating well with decreased erythema. Repeat imaging was not performed as clinical improvement was clear.

He was discharged on January 26, 2026 (hospital day 4) on oral ciprofloxacin 500 mg twice daily plus metronidazole 500 mg three times daily for an additional 10 days. Wound care instructions included daily packing changes with saline-moistened gauze. He was given a follow-up appointment with Dr. Okonkwo at Heartland Surgical Associates for February 5, 2026.

**Final Surgeon Follow-Up (February 5, 2026):** James returned to Heartland Surgical Associates for his post-readmission follow-up. He was afebrile, feeling well, and had returned to light duty at work. The port site wound had healed by secondary intention with good granulation and no residual erythema or drainage. All other port site incisions were well-healed. Dr. Okonkwo documented the wound as healed, discontinued antibiotics (course already completed), and cleared James to resume full work duties in 2 weeks. No further follow-up was needed unless symptoms recurred.

James has no active medications. His antibiotic courses are completed. He uses occasional over-the-counter acetaminophen for minor aches. His oxycodone prescription was used minimally (he reports taking only 4 of the 20 tablets) and has been disposed of.

## Demo Relevance

James Thornton's record is purpose-built to demonstrate **UC5: Payer Claims Adjudication** in the SMART Permission Tickets reference implementation. Here is how his data exercises each access constraint dimension:

### Scopes (Resource-Type Filtering)

The payer's Permission Ticket requests access to a narrow set of FHIR resource types relevant to claims review — not the patient's entire chart. Specifically:

- **DocumentReference** — operative report, pathology report, discharge summaries, ED notes, radiology reports
- **Procedure** — laparoscopic appendectomy, abscess drainage
- **DiagnosticReport** — surgical pathology, CT imaging reports, wound culture
- **Condition** — perforated appendicitis, surgical site infection
- **Encounter** — to establish dates of service and encounter types
- **MedicationRequest** — antibiotic courses (relevant to SSI treatment verification)

The ticket should *not* grant access to the full breadth of James's record (e.g., historical rotator cuff records from 2021, immunization history, social history screening). This tests the system's ability to enforce resource-type scoping.

### Periods (Temporal Filtering)

The Permission Ticket specifies a narrow time window: **January 12, 2026 through February 5, 2026** — the complete surgical episode from initial ED presentation through final post-operative clearance. This excludes:

- The 2021 rotator cuff encounters at Heartland Surgical Associates
- Any prior primary care or pharmacy records
- Any future encounters

This tests the system's ability to enforce period-based access constraints, ensuring the payer receives only data from the relevant clinical episode.

### Organizations (Site Filtering)

The ticket authorizes access to data held by two specific organizations:

- **Nebraska Medicine** (NPI: 1437289650) — where the surgical care and inpatient stays occurred
- **Heartland Surgical Associates** (NPI: 1659482073) — where outpatient follow-up occurred

Both sites are in Nebraska, so there is no cross-state jurisdictional complexity. The simplicity is intentional: UC5 focuses on payer access patterns rather than cross-jurisdictional challenges.

### Requester Identity

The requester is an **Organization** (Blue Cross Blue Shield of Nebraska, NPI: 1104867235), not an individual practitioner or the patient themselves. This tests the system's ability to handle organizational requesters with a legitimate payer relationship, including:

- Subject resolution by reference (James is already a known patient at both data-holder sites)
- Requester validation as a recognized payer entity
- Purpose of use: claims adjudication (HCPQ payment operations)

### Claim Context

The Permission Ticket includes claim-level context:

- **Service:** Appendectomy (SNOMED CT: 80146002 — Excision of appendix)
- **Claim identifiers** linking the access request to specific claims under review
- **Complication narrative:** The SSI and readmission make this a non-routine claim. The payer needs to verify that the readmission was medically necessary and related to the index surgery, not an unrelated admission. This is a common real-world claims adjudication pattern.

### Data Footprint

James's record is intentionally compact. With only 5–7 encounters across two sites over a 25-day window, the total FHIR resource volume is approximately 460–580 resources. This makes the dataset easy to inspect manually during demos while still containing enough clinical richness (operative report, pathology, imaging, wound culture, two hospitalizations, SSI complication) to exercise the Permission Ticket machinery meaningfully.
