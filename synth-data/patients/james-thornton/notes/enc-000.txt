ED COURSE AND MEDICAL DECISION MAKING:
This is a 47-year-old otherwise healthy male presenting with a classic history of appendicitis — periumbilical pain migrating to the RLQ over several hours with associated nausea, fever, and tachycardia. Physical exam is notable for RLQ tenderness with guarding, rebound, and positive McBurney and Rovsing signs, highly suggestive of acute appendicitis. Laboratory evaluation demonstrates significant leukocytosis with neutrophilic predominance and elevated CRP, supporting an acute inflammatory/infectious process. Urinalysis was negative, effectively ruling out urinary tract pathology. Lipase was normal, excluding pancreatitis.

CT imaging confirmed the clinical suspicion and further revealed perforation with extraluminal gas and a 2.3 cm periappendiceal abscess, indicating complicated appendicitis requiring emergent surgical intervention.

The patient was resuscitated with normal saline 1000 mL IV bolus. Pain was managed with morphine sulfate 4 mg IV x1 with improvement in symptoms. Ondansetron 4 mg IV x1 was administered for nausea with good effect.
