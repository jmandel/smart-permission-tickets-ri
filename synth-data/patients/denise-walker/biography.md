# Denise Walker

## Patient Overview

Denise Walker is a 58-year-old Black woman living in Albuquerque, New Mexico. She is English-speaking, non-Hispanic, and recently widowed after many years of marriage. She spent most of her adult life in Phoenix, Arizona, where she worked as a school district transportation dispatcher, a job that suited her organized personality and comfort juggling complicated schedules. In early 2024, after her husband's death and with her own health becoming harder to manage alone, she moved from Arizona to Albuquerque to live closer to her adult daughter and help with her grandchildren. She is covered through a Medicare Advantage plan. Denise has high health literacy, keeps folders of visit summaries and medication lists, and usually understands why each treatment is prescribed, but transportation gaps, pharmacy cost swings, and the burden of coordinating several specialists have still led to occasional delays in follow-up and short interruptions in medication access.

## Clinical Arc

Denise's health story is one of steady accumulation rather than a single catastrophic event. She was diagnosed with type 2 diabetes mellitus in her early 40s, around 2010, after several years of increasing weight, fatigue, and routine labs showing fasting glucose in the diabetic range. At first her diabetes was managed with lifestyle counseling and metformin, but over time her control became harder to maintain. During the mid-2010s she also developed essential hypertension and hyperlipidemia, both treated in primary care. For several years she did reasonably well with regular office visits, home blood-pressure checks, and periodic laboratory monitoring, though her hemoglobin A1c generally ran above target, often in the high 7% to low 9% range.

By roughly 2018 to 2020, the burden of chronic disease became more visible. She began describing burning foot pain, numbness at night, and reduced sensation in both feet, and was diagnosed with diabetic peripheral neuropathy. Around the same period, urine testing started to show persistent albuminuria, and serum creatinine crept upward enough that her primary care clinician began documenting early chronic kidney disease. Her kidney function was not initially unstable, but the trend was clear: a creatinine that had once been closer to normal gradually moved into the range of about 1.3 to 1.6 mg/dL, with estimated GFR declining into stage 3 chronic kidney disease territory. Her blood pressure became more difficult to control, making renal protection an important theme of care.

Cardiovascular disease entered the picture more dramatically in Arizona. In 2021 she developed exertional dyspnea, ankle swelling, and reduced exercise tolerance. Cardiology evaluation, including echocardiography, showed heart failure with reduced ejection fraction, with left ventricular ejection fraction documented in the mid-30% range. Not long afterward, she also had paroxysmal atrial fibrillation recognized after episodes of palpitations and lightheadedness, sometimes captured on ECG and sometimes inferred from symptom-triggered monitoring. Those diagnoses changed the tenor of her care: she was no longer managing diabetes and blood pressure alone, but balancing volume status, rhythm control strategy, anticoagulation, renal safety, and medication adherence across several prescribers.

Her Arizona clinicians gradually assembled a stable, evidence-based regimen. Metformin was eventually stopped as kidney disease progressed and because of GI intolerance during periods of poor appetite. Basal insulin was added and titrated, settling into insulin glargine as a long-term medication. Empagliflozin became an important part of both diabetes and heart-failure management. For heart failure and atrial fibrillation, she was treated with carvedilol, sacubitril/valsartan, loop diuretic therapy with furosemide, and apixaban for stroke prevention. Atorvastatin remained part of her chronic prevention regimen. There were points where medication lists changed quickly, especially after cardiology visits, but by late 2023 her active long-term medications were recognizably consistent: insulin glargine, empagliflozin, carvedilol, sacubitril/valsartan, furosemide, apixaban, and atorvastatin, with intermittent adjunctive agents depending on blood-pressure readings and symptoms. She has no known drug-induced anaphylaxis, but her chart consistently records lisinopril as causing a troublesome cough, and she reports shellfish causing hives. Those details matter because they influenced medication choices and are likely to appear in allergy and intolerance data.

The hardest stretch came while caring for her husband during his final illness in 2023 and early 2024. Denise missed some of her own appointments, ate irregularly, and described stress-related worsening of glucose control. During that period her A1c rose to around 9.4% on Arizona labs, blood pressures were less consistent, and refill timing became uneven. After her husband's death she relocated to Albuquerque. The move itself introduced a brief but clinically meaningful gap: she had several weeks of disrupted medication access while establishing new clinicians, transferring prescriptions, and navigating a new insurance and pharmacy setup. That interruption likely contributed to fluid retention and symptom worsening soon after arrival.

In New Mexico, her first major event was an acute decompensated heart-failure hospitalization at University of New Mexico Hospital in 2024. She presented with progressive shortness of breath, orthopnea, leg edema, and weight gain after the move, and the admission workup showed volume overload on top of known HFrEF. Hospital data would reasonably include ED evaluation, inpatient progress notes, repeat echocardiography, ECGs showing atrial fibrillation history or sinus rhythm with intermittent ectopy, chest imaging, BNP elevation, renal function monitoring, and aggressive diuresis. Her creatinine rose transiently during decongestion but improved enough for discharge planning. That hospitalization became the pivot point for the New Mexico chapter of her care.

After discharge, Denise entered more structured specialty follow-up. The UNM Heart Failure Clinic helped titrate her carvedilol and sacubitril/valsartan to tolerable doses, reinforced daily weights and sodium restriction, and monitored recurrent symptoms, renal function, and potassium. Rio Grande Nephrology Associates followed her chronic kidney disease, now best characterized as CKD stage 3b with persistent albuminuria, focusing on BP targets, volume balance, medication safety, and diabetic kidney disease progression. Typical outpatient nephrology labs for Denise include creatinine around 1.5 to 1.8 mg/dL, eGFR roughly 32 to 40 mL/min/1.73 m2, urine albumin-creatinine ratio in the few-hundred-mg/g range, and potassium values watched closely while she remains on guideline-directed heart-failure therapy.

Her diabetes complications also became more visible after the move. Retinal screening and then retina specialty care in Albuquerque documented diabetic retinopathy with macular edema. Sandia Retina Specialists would hold the most disease-specific ophthalmic data in her chart: retinal imaging, OCT measurements, detailed eye findings, procedure notes, and intravitreal injection records. Those eye visits are clinically separate from the rest of her chronic disease management, which makes them especially useful for organization- and scope-based access demonstrations.

Despite the complexity, Denise remains engaged in her care. She uses a pill organizer, tracks morning weights in a notebook, and can usually describe what each medication is for. Her diabetes control has improved modestly since re-establishing continuity in New Mexico, with A1c trending down into roughly the low 8% range rather than the high 9% range seen during bereavement and relocation. Her heart failure symptoms are still present but better controlled than at the time of hospitalization, usually NYHA class II to III depending on volume status. Her atrial fibrillation remains paroxysmal rather than permanently uncontrolled, and anticoagulation has continued without a known thromboembolic event. Chronic kidney disease remains active and important, but stable enough for outpatient management. Her long-term chart would also show routine adult immunizations received through primary care, including annual influenza vaccination, COVID-19 primary series with at least one booster, pneumococcal vaccination appropriate for chronic disease risk, Tdap within the past decade, and recombinant zoster vaccination completed in adulthood.

## Demo Relevance

Denise Walker is a strong SMART Permission Tickets reference patient because her data naturally spans multiple clinically meaningful boundaries without feeling artificial.

For `jurisdiction`, her record separates cleanly into Arizona care and New Mexico care. Arizona contains the longer baseline story of diabetes, hypertension, early CKD, and initial cardiology management. New Mexico contains the post-move deterioration, heart-failure hospitalization, specialty intensification, nephrology monitoring, and retina procedures. A ticket could therefore authorize only Arizona records, only New Mexico records, or both.

For `period`, her chart supports at least three distinct windows. One period is the pre-move chronic baseline through March 2024. A second is the high-risk transition around the relocation and heart-failure hospitalization in spring 2024. A third is the more stable but still active longitudinal follow-up afterward. That makes it possible to demonstrate how time-bounded access changes the visible story.

For `organization`, Denise's care is well partitioned. Desert Family Medicine holds the broad primary-care longitudinal record. Valley Heart Institute holds Arizona cardiology and echo-driven treatment changes. University of New Mexico Hospital and Heart Failure Clinic holds the acute hospitalization and recent heart-failure management. Rio Grande Nephrology Associates holds CKD staging and renal monitoring. Sandia Retina Specialists holds diabetic eye disease findings and intravitreal treatment. Organization-level authorization would therefore surface substantially different slices of her chart rather than redundant copies.

For `scopes`, Denise's record supports both broad and narrow demonstrations. Full-chart access would expose Conditions, MedicationRequests, Observations, DiagnosticReports, Procedures, Immunizations, Encounters, and DocumentReferences across all sites. Narrower access could focus only on renal and cardiac monitoring resources, only ophthalmology-related resources, only medication and condition data, or only inpatient versus outpatient records. Because several chronic problems evolve over time rather than appearing as one-off diagnoses, she is also useful for showing how authorized data can reveal longitudinal updates to CKD stage, heart-failure status, atrial-fibrillation management, and glycemic control instead of duplicate snapshots.

In practical demo terms, Denise exercises all four major access-constraint dimensions at once: multi-year timeline, two-state jurisdiction split, five-organization partitioning, and clinically meaningful resource scoping. She is complex enough to feel realistic, but structured enough that downstream FHIR generation can create coherent trends in labs, medications, procedures, and notes across sites.
