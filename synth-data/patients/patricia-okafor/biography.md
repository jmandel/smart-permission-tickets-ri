## Clinical Arc

### Baseline Health (2018–2023)

Patricia established care at Westheimer Family Medicine in 2018 after her previous PCP retired. At intake, her active problems were **essential hypertension** (diagnosed ~2010, well-controlled on lisinopril 10 mg daily) and **prediabetes** (HbA1c 5.9%, managed with diet and exercise). She was otherwise in good health. Her BMI hovered around 27–28, blood pressure was consistently 128–134/78–82 mmHg on medication, and her metabolic panels were unremarkable. She received annual flu vaccines, a pneumococcal vaccine (PCV20) in 2022, and a COVID-19 primary series plus boosters.

Her 2022 annual wellness visit was routine: vitals stable, PHQ-2 negative, HbA1c 6.0% (still prediabetic range), lipid panel mildly elevated (LDL 138). Dr. Adebayo discussed statin therapy but Patricia preferred to intensify dietary changes. Screening mammogram and colonoscopy were up to date.

Her 2023 annual visit was similarly unremarkable. HbA1c held at 6.0%, blood pressure 130/80, BMI 27.4. She reported occasional mild knee pain attributed to osteoarthritis. Dr. Adebayo started her on as-needed naproxen. Low-dose chest CT for lung cancer screening was not performed — Patricia did not meet USPSTF criteria as she was a never-smoker, and secondhand smoke exposure alone was not a standard indication.

### The Diagnostic Journey (July–October 2024)

In July 2024, Patricia called Westheimer Family Medicine reporting a persistent dry cough that had been present for about six weeks. She had initially attributed it to seasonal allergies, but it was not responding to over-the-counter antihistamines. Dr. Adebayo saw her for an acute visit on July 18, 2024. On exam, lungs were clear to auscultation, oxygen saturation was 96% on room air, and she was afebrile. He prescribed a short course of benzonatate and scheduled follow-up in two weeks.

At the August 1 follow-up, the cough persisted. Patricia also mentioned she had lost about 8 pounds over the past two months without trying — she attributed it to stress about her daughter's divorce, but Dr. Adebayo was concerned. He ordered a chest X-ray, which showed a suspicious opacity in the right upper lobe. He also checked a CBC (hemoglobin 11.8 g/dL, mildly low) and a comprehensive metabolic panel (unremarkable except albumin 3.4, mildly low). He urgently referred her to Houston Pulmonary Associates for further evaluation and ordered a CT chest with contrast.

**Dr. Adebayo's assessment at that visit:** persistent cough, unintentional weight loss, right upper lobe opacity — lung malignancy must be excluded.

### Pulmonary Workup (August–September 2024)

Patricia was seen at Houston Pulmonary Associates by Dr. Nisha Ramaswamy on August 12, 2024. The CT chest with contrast (performed August 8) showed a **4.2 cm spiculated mass in the right upper lobe** with **ipsilateral mediastinal lymphadenopathy** (stations 4R and 7, largest node 2.1 cm). No pleural effusion. Dr. Ramaswamy performed pulmonary function testing on August 19: FEV1 was 78% predicted, FVC 82% predicted, DLCO 71% predicted — mild restrictive pattern with reduced diffusion capacity. She discussed the findings with Patricia and Emeka, explaining the high suspicion for lung cancer, and referred her urgently to MD Anderson Cancer Center for staging and tissue diagnosis.

A follow-up telephone encounter on September 3 confirmed the MD Anderson referral was processed and Patricia's appointment was scheduled. Dr. Ramaswamy also prescribed a short course of guaifenesin with codeine for nighttime cough management.

### Staging and Diagnosis at MD Anderson (September–November 2024)

Patricia was first seen at MD Anderson Cancer Center on September 16, 2024, by Dr. Carlos Menendez in the Thoracic Oncology clinic. The initial visit included a comprehensive history and physical, review of outside imaging, and a plan for staging workup.

Over the next several weeks, the following was completed:

- **PET/CT (September 23, 2024):** FDG-avid right upper lobe mass (SUVmax 12.4) with FDG-avid ipsilateral mediastinal nodes (SUVmax 8.1 at station 4R). No distant metastases. Adrenal glands normal. No osseous lesions.
- **Brain MRI with contrast (September 25, 2024):** No intracranial metastases.
- **Endobronchial ultrasound-guided transbronchial needle aspiration (EBUS-TBNA) (October 2, 2024):** Sampled station 4R and 7 nodes. Pathology confirmed **adenocarcinoma** in both stations. Immunohistochemistry: TTF-1 positive, CK7 positive, CK20 negative, p40 negative — consistent with pulmonary origin.
- **Molecular testing (October 10, 2024):** Next-generation sequencing panel — **EGFR wild-type**, **ALK rearrangement negative**, **ROS1 negative**, **BRAF V600E negative**, **KRAS G12C positive** (though not targetable in first-line at the time of enrollment), **PD-L1 TPS 60%** by 22C3 assay.

**Final staging: Stage IIIA (T2b N2 M0) non-small cell lung cancer, adenocarcinoma subtype.**

Dr. Menendez discussed treatment options with Patricia and Emeka at a dedicated goals-of-care visit on October 15. Given her PD-L1 high expression, good performance status (ECOG 1), and stage IIIA disease, she was an excellent candidate for a clinical trial.

### Clinical Trial Enrollment (November–December 2024)

Dr. Menendez presented Patricia with the opportunity to participate in a **Phase III randomized clinical trial (NCT05820295)** comparing **pembrolizumab + carboplatin/pemetrexed** versus **carboplatin/pemetrexed alone** as first-line treatment for unresectable stage III NSCLC. The trial's primary endpoint was progression-free survival.

Patricia had a screening visit on November 4, 2024, which included repeat labs (CBC, CMP, LDH, thyroid function — all acceptable for enrollment), an ECG (normal sinus rhythm), and an echocardiogram (LVEF 62%, no valvular abnormalities). She met all inclusion criteria and no exclusion criteria.

She signed **informed consent** on December 2, 2024, after discussing the trial with her family, her pastor, and Dr. Menendez. The research coordinator, Jennifer Liu, RN, completed enrollment paperwork and baseline quality-of-life assessments (EORTC QLQ-C30, QLQ-LC13).

As part of the research protocol, MD Anderson's research team requested access to Patricia's medical records from Westheimer Family Medicine and Houston Pulmonary Associates for the period **January 2022 through present**, to correlate treatment response with prior health status, comorbidity burden, and the diagnostic trajectory.

### Treatment Course (January 2025–Present)

Patricia began **Cycle 1** of chemo-immunotherapy on January 6, 2025:
- **Pembrolizumab** 200 mg IV every 3 weeks
- **Carboplatin** AUC 5 IV every 3 weeks
- **Pemetrexed** 500 mg/m² IV every 3 weeks

She tolerated cycle 1 reasonably well, with grade 1 nausea managed with ondansetron and grade 1 fatigue. She continued her daily walks, though reduced to one mile. Her cough began to improve after cycle 2.

**Cycle 2 (January 27, 2025):** Labs showed ANC 1.8 (acceptable), hemoglobin 10.9, platelets 156K. Treatment administered on schedule.

**Cycle 3 (February 17, 2025):** Developed grade 2 fatigue and mild peripheral neuropathy (numbness in fingertips). Carboplatin dose reduced by 25%. Added gabapentin 300 mg at bedtime for neuropathy.

**Cycle 4 (March 10, 2025):** Labs stable. Neuropathy improved with gabapentin. Fatigue persistent but manageable.

**Interim imaging (March 24, 2025):** CT chest/abdomen/pelvis showed **partial response** — primary mass decreased from 4.2 cm to 2.8 cm, mediastinal nodes decreased in size. No new lesions. RECIST 1.1 assessment: partial response.

Patricia transitioned to **maintenance pembrolizumab** monotherapy in April 2025, continuing every 3 weeks. Carboplatin and pemetrexed were discontinued per protocol after 4 cycles. She is tolerating maintenance well, with ongoing grade 1 fatigue and stable neuropathy.

## Demo Relevance

### Use Case Exercised: UC6 — Research Study Consent

Patricia's scenario demonstrates a **research institution (MD Anderson) using a Permission Ticket to access a patient's historical records from community providers**, with the patient's informed consent, to support a clinical trial. This is a core Permission Ticket use case where:

- The **requester** is an **Organization** (MD Anderson Cancer Center / research institute), not an individual clinician
- The **subject** is resolved by **identifier** (Patricia's MRN at each community site — she may have MRN 4820193 at Westheimer Family Medicine and MRN 7103482 at Houston Pulmonary Associates)
- The **basis** is patient consent for research participation (informed consent signed December 2, 2024)
- The **details** include the condition motivating access (lung cancer — SNOMED 254637007 "Non-small cell lung cancer") and the study identifier (NCT05820295)

### Permission Ticket Constraint Dimensions Demonstrated

**1. Scopes (Resource Type Constraints)**
The research protocol defines which FHIR resource types are needed. The Permission Ticket should authorize access to:
- `Condition` — comorbidities, cancer diagnosis, treatment side effects
- `Observation` — vitals, labs, PFT results, screening scores
- `DiagnosticReport` — imaging reports, pathology, PFTs
- `MedicationRequest` — medication history
- `Procedure` — procedures performed at community sites
- `DocumentReference` — clinical notes (for NLP extraction of symptoms, clinical reasoning)

Notably **excluded** from research access: financial data, psychotherapy notes, substance abuse records, and any resources not specified in the study protocol. This demonstrates that Permission Tickets can be **scoped narrowly by resource type** even when the time window is broad.

**2. Periods (Temporal Constraints)**
The research protocol requests data from **January 1, 2022 through the present**. This 3+ year window:
- Captures the pre-diagnosis baseline (2022–mid 2024): routine chronic disease management, normal screening, stable vitals
- Captures the diagnostic journey (July–October 2024): the cough, weight loss, imaging, referrals
- Captures the peri-enrollment period (October–December 2024): staging results that exist at MD Anderson but may have been communicated to community providers
- Excludes older historical data (pre-2022) that is not relevant to the study — Patricia's 2018–2021 records at Westheimer are **not** authorized for research access

This demonstrates that Permission Tickets can enforce **temporal boundaries** that differ from what a treating clinician might access.

**3. Jurisdictions (Geographic/Legal Constraints)**
All three sites are in **Texas**, so this scenario operates within a single state jurisdiction. This simplifies the jurisdictional dimension but still demonstrates:
- Texas-specific consent requirements for research use of medical records
- HIPAA research authorization (45 CFR 164.508) as the legal basis
- The Permission Ticket encoding that the consent is valid under Texas law

A future extension could involve Patricia's daughter in Atlanta requesting records from Georgia, introducing cross-state jurisdictional complexity.

**4. Organizations (Multi-Organization Access)**
The Permission Ticket must identify:
- **Requesting organization:** MD Anderson Cancer Center (NPI 1275689043)
- **Target organizations:** Westheimer Family Medicine (NPI 1326847590) and Houston Pulmonary Associates (NPI 1548273016)
- **Patient identity at each target:** Patricia's MRN at each site

This demonstrates the **multi-organization** dimension — a single Permission Ticket (or a set of coordinated tickets) enabling a research institute to pull records from multiple community providers on behalf of a consented patient.

### What Makes This Patient Interesting for the Demo

1. **Diagnostic odyssey across sites:** The journey from "persistent cough at PCP" → "suspicious CT at pulmonologist" → "confirmed cancer at MD Anderson" creates a natural multi-site narrative that research access must span.

2. **Rich data variety:** Vitals, labs, imaging, pathology, molecular testing, infusion records, PFTs, QOL instruments — this patient generates diverse FHIR resource types across all three sites.

3. **Clinical trial context:** The ResearchStudy and ResearchSubject resources at MD Anderson, combined with the informed consent, provide the legal and clinical basis for the Permission Ticket.

4. **Temporal boundary testing:** The 2022–present window is meaningful — it includes enough pre-diagnosis data to establish baselines, captures the full diagnostic arc, but intentionally excludes older records. A system that incorrectly returns 2018–2021 data would be violating the ticket's constraints.

5. **Realistic scale:** ~10–14 encounters total across three sites, generating approximately 800–1000 FHIR resources in aggregate — enough to be meaningful for testing but manageable for demo purposes.
