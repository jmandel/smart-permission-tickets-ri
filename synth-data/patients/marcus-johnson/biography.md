## Clinical Arc

### Early Childhood and Immunization Gap (2004–2010)

Marcus was born at Froedtert Hospital in Milwaukee on June 14, 2004, a healthy full-term infant weighing 7 lbs 12 oz. His pediatric care was managed at a clinic in Milwaukee through early childhood. He received his first MMR vaccine at 12 months of age (June 2005) per the standard schedule. However, between roughly 2008 and 2011, his parents — influenced by anti-vaccine messaging circulating at the time — became hesitant about additional vaccinations. As a result, Marcus missed his second MMR dose, which is normally given between ages 4 and 6. His parents eventually returned to the standard immunization schedule around age 7, catching him up on Tdap, IPV boosters, and other routine vaccines, but the second MMR was never specifically revisited or flagged. This gap would remain clinically silent for over fifteen years.

### Adolescence (2010–2022)

Marcus grew up healthy in Milwaukee. He played basketball in high school and had the usual minor visits — a sprained ankle at age 16, annual sports physicals. No hospitalizations, no surgeries, no significant illnesses. He had seasonal allergies (mild, managed with OTC loratadine occasionally) but no drug allergies and no chronic conditions. His BMI stayed in the normal range (22–23). He received a meningococcal conjugate booster at age 16 and a Tdap booster at age 15, both documented through his Milwaukee pediatrician.

### College Years at UW–Madison (2023–2026)

Marcus enrolled at UW–Madison in the fall of 2023. He established care at UW Health University Student Health Services on campus, as required for enrollment. His records from Milwaukee were not fully transferred — the student health center created a new chart based on his self-reported history and the immunization records his mother provided, which listed only the single MMR dose. The intake nurse noted the incomplete MMR series but it was not flagged for immediate follow-up, as the student was otherwise healthy and the visit was focused on a general wellness check.

**Encounter 1 — New Patient / Wellness Visit (September 12, 2023):** Marcus presented for his initial student health visit. Vitals were normal: BP 118/72, HR 68, temp 98.4°F, weight 176 lbs, height 5'11", BMI 24.5, SpO2 99%. A PHQ-2 depression screen was negative (score 0). The clinician, Dr. Rachel Okonkwo, reviewed his immunization history, noted the single MMR dose, and documented "incomplete MMR series — consider catch-up." A standard metabolic panel was not drawn. The visit generated a progress note and standard vital signs observations.

**Encounter 2 — Acute Visit / Upper Respiratory Infection (January 22, 2025):** Marcus came in with 3 days of nasal congestion, sore throat, and mild cough. Vitals: BP 120/74, HR 76, temp 99.1°F, weight 178 lbs, SpO2 98%. Rapid strep test was negative. Diagnosed with acute viral upper respiratory infection (SNOMED 54150009). Advised rest, fluids, and OTC acetaminophen. No antibiotics prescribed. He recovered uneventfully within a week.

**Encounter 3 — STI Screening (October 8, 2025):** Marcus requested routine STI screening. Vitals were taken per clinic protocol: BP 116/70, HR 64, temp 98.2°F, weight 180 lbs, SpO2 99%. Chlamydia/gonorrhea NAAT (urine) and HIV 4th-generation antigen/antibody screen were ordered. All results returned negative. Brief sexual health counseling was provided by the clinic nurse.

### The Measles Episode (March 2026)

Marcus traveled to Manila, Philippines, for spring break (March 7–15, 2026) to visit a college friend's family. The Philippines was experiencing an active measles outbreak at the time, with clusters reported in Metro Manila and surrounding provinces. Marcus was unaware of his incomplete MMR vaccination status and did not seek pre-travel medical advice.

He returned to Madison on March 15, 2026. He felt well for the first several days and resumed his normal routine — attending lectures in large halls (CS 400 with ~250 students, Math 340 with ~180 students), eating in the Gordon Dining Hall, and working out at the Nick Recreation Center.

**Prodrome (March 20–22, 2026):** Marcus began feeling unwell on March 20 with malaise, loss of appetite, and a low-grade fever. By March 21 he had a worsening cough, runny nose (coryza), and red, watery eyes (conjunctivitis). He assumed he had caught the flu and took OTC acetaminophen and ibuprofen.

**Encounter 4 — Index Measles Visit (March 23, 2026):** On March 23, with a fever of 104.1°F and a spreading rash that had started on his face and was moving down to his neck and trunk, Marcus presented to UW Student Health Services. He was triaged urgently. Vitals: BP 110/68, HR 108, temp 104.1°F (40.1°C), RR 20, weight 175 lbs (he had lost a few pounds from poor appetite), SpO2 97%. The examining clinician, Dr. Rachel Okonkwo, immediately noted the classic triad of cough, coryza, and conjunctivitis, and on oral examination observed Koplik spots — small white lesions on an erythematous base on the buccal mucosa opposite the molars. The maculopapular rash was confluent on the face and discrete on the trunk and upper arms (spreading cephalocaudally, consistent with measles).

Marcus was immediately placed in airborne isolation. Dr. Okonkwo ordered:
- **Measles IgM antibody** (serum) — returned **positive** (March 24)
- **Measles RNA PCR** (nasopharyngeal swab) — returned **positive** (March 25)
- **CBC with differential** — showed leukopenia (WBC 3.2 × 10³/µL), lymphocyte count low
- **CMP** — within normal limits except mildly elevated AST (52 U/L)
- **CRP** — elevated at 48 mg/L

Diagnosis: **Measles (SNOMED 14189004)**, confirmed by laboratory evidence and clinical presentation. The case was immediately reportable. Dr. Okonkwo filed a report to **Public Health Madison & Dane County** and the **Wisconsin Department of Health Services (DHS)** on the same day per Wisconsin Statute 252. The case was assigned state case ID **WI-MEAS-2026-0047**.

Marcus was advised to isolate in a single dorm room (he was moved to a designated isolation room in University Housing). He was prescribed supportive care: acetaminophen 500 mg every 6 hours as needed for fever, oral rehydration, and rest. No antiviral therapy was indicated. Vitamin A supplementation (200,000 IU × 2 doses) was administered per WHO recommendation for measles in young adults.

**Encounter 5 — Telehealth Follow-up During Isolation (March 26, 2026):** A telehealth video visit was conducted by nurse practitioner Amy Liu, NP, to check on Marcus during isolation. He reported his fever had decreased to 101°F, the rash had spread to his extremities but was beginning to fade on his face, and his cough was persisting but improving. He was tolerating fluids and soft foods. No signs of complications (pneumonia, encephalitis) were noted. He was advised to continue isolation through March 27 (4 days after rash onset) and to return for in-person follow-up thereafter.

**Encounter 6 — Post-Recovery Clearance Visit (March 30, 2026):** Marcus returned to the student health center for a clearance visit. His fever had resolved (temp 98.6°F). The rash had faded to brownish discoloration with fine desquamation. Vitals: BP 116/72, HR 72, temp 98.6°F, SpO2 99%. Repeat CBC showed recovering leukocyte count (WBC 5.8 × 10³/µL). CRP had dropped to 8 mg/L. He was cleared to return to classes and communal living. Dr. Okonkwo documented that his natural measles infection now conferred immunity, and that the second MMR dose was no longer indicated. A follow-up note was sent to the Wisconsin DHS confirming recovery.

### Public Health Response

The confirmation of measles in a college student who had attended large lectures, dined in communal facilities, and used a public gym during his infectious period (estimated March 19–27, 2026) triggered a significant public health response:

- **Contact tracing:** Public Health Madison & Dane County identified over 800 potentially exposed individuals across three large lecture halls, one dining hall, and the campus recreation center.
- **Exposure notifications:** UW–Madison issued a campus-wide health alert. Students and staff who could not verify two MMR doses were advised to receive post-exposure prophylaxis (MMR vaccine within 72 hours or immunoglobulin within 6 days).
- **Immunization review:** The university conducted an emergency review of immunization compliance records, identifying 23 other students with incomplete MMR series.
- **No secondary cases** were ultimately identified, likely due to high background vaccination rates in the campus population and rapid public health response.

The Wisconsin DHS requested Marcus's clinical records from UW Student Health Services via a Permission Ticket to support the investigation — specifically his immunization history, encounter records from the infectious period, diagnostic results, and condition documentation.

## Demo Relevance

Marcus Johnson's scenario demonstrates **Use Case 3 (UC3): Public Health Investigation** in the SMART Permission Tickets reference implementation. This is a measles-specific variant that contrasts with slower-moving investigations (like tuberculosis) by featuring an acute, explosive exposure event with urgent public health implications.

### Permission Ticket Access Constraint Dimensions Exercised

**1. Scopes (Resource-Type Constraints)**
The Wisconsin DHS Permission Ticket requests access to specific FHIR resource types relevant to the measles investigation — not Marcus's full medical record. The ticket would authorize:

- **Condition** — to confirm the measles diagnosis (SNOMED 14189004) and its clinical timeline
- **DiagnosticReport** and **Observation** — to review measles IgM, measles RNA PCR, and supporting labs (CBC, CRP)
- **Immunization** — critically important to document the incomplete MMR series (single dose at 12 months, missing second dose), which explains susceptibility and informs the broader campus immunization review
- **Encounter** — to establish the clinical timeline of the index visit, isolation, and follow-up
- **DocumentReference** — progress notes from the measles-related encounters

The ticket would explicitly **exclude** access to unrelated clinical data such as the STI screening results (October 2025) or the routine URI visit (January 2025), which are not relevant to the public health investigation.

**2. Temporal Period Constraints**
The Permission Ticket is constrained to a specific investigation window. The relevant period is approximately **March 15, 2026 – March 30, 2026** (from return from the Philippines through post-recovery clearance), though immunization history reaching back to childhood is also in scope. This demonstrates how a Permission Ticket can combine a narrow encounter-period window with broader historical access for specific resource types (immunizations).

**3. Requester as Organization (Jurisdictional Authority)**
The requester is the **Wisconsin Department of Health Services** — a government organization, not an individual clinician. This exercises the Permission Ticket pattern where:
- The requesting entity is an `Organization` resource with `type = govt`
- The authority derives from state public health statute (Wis. Stat. § 252), not from a patient consent or treatment relationship
- The `detail` field on the ticket includes the reportable condition (measles, SNOMED 14189004) and the state case identifier (WI-MEAS-2026-0047)

**4. Subject Resolution by Reference**
Marcus is a known patient at UW Student Health Services (he has an existing Patient resource and MRN). The Permission Ticket identifies him by direct reference to his Patient resource at the UW Health FHIR endpoint, rather than requiring demographic matching. This demonstrates the simpler subject-resolution path.

### What Makes This Scenario Distinctive

- **Acute timeline:** The entire clinical episode spans ~10 days, unlike chronic disease scenarios. The Permission Ticket's temporal scope is compact but the public health stakes are enormous.
- **Immunization gap as investigation focus:** The missing second MMR dose is the clinical finding that explains Marcus's susceptibility. Public health needs immunization records to understand why a vaccinated-age adult contracted measles — this is a primary justification for the Permission Ticket's immunization scope.
- **Small data footprint, high criticality:** Marcus has only ~6 encounters and ~250–350 FHIR resources at a single site. But every measles-related data point is critical to the investigation. This contrasts with scenarios involving large multi-site records where the challenge is filtering volume.
- **Single clinical site:** Only one provider (UW Student Health) holds relevant records, simplifying the multi-site federation aspect but focusing the demo on scope and period constraints within a single Permission Ticket.
- **Campus exposure scale:** While the clinical data is compact, the public health context (800+ exposed contacts, campus-wide alert) illustrates why timely, authorized access to clinical records matters for outbreak response.
