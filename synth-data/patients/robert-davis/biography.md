## Clinical Arc

### Background Chronic Conditions (2010–2025)

Robert's chronic health problems developed over the course of his fifties and early sixties, managed through intermittent primary care visits at a community health center near his home.

**Hypertension** was first diagnosed around 2010 during a walk-in visit for a sinus infection. His blood pressure was 158/94 that day. He was started on lisinopril 10 mg daily. Over the years the dose was titrated up to 20 mg. His blood pressure has generally been controlled in the 130s/80s range, though it runs higher when he skips doses or eats too much salt.

**Type 2 diabetes mellitus** was diagnosed in 2015 after a routine HbA1c came back at 7.8%. He was started on metformin 500 mg twice daily, later increased to 1000 mg twice daily. His A1c has fluctuated between 7.0% and 8.2%. He checks his blood sugar at home sporadically. He has no diabetic retinopathy or neuropathy documented yet, though he has not had a dilated eye exam in over two years.

**Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), mild** was diagnosed in 2017 after pulmonary function testing showed an FEV1 of 72% predicted with a reduced FEV1/FVC ratio. He uses an albuterol inhaler as needed, maybe once or twice a week. He has not required hospitalization for a COPD exacerbation. He was counseled on smoking cessation at diagnosis and proudly notes he had already quit two years earlier.

**Other history:** Osteoarthritis of the left knee, managed with occasional acetaminophen. Seasonal allergies. He received a pneumococcal vaccine (PCV20) in 2023 and gets a flu shot most years, though he has not received any COVID-19 boosters since his initial series in 2021.

**Allergies:** No known drug allergies. Reports mild seasonal rhinitis with ragweed.

**Medications (pre-TB diagnosis):**
- Lisinopril 20 mg daily
- Metformin 1000 mg twice daily
- Albuterol HFA inhaler PRN
- Acetaminophen 500 mg PRN for knee pain

### The TB Episode (January–April 2026)

In late December 2025, Robert developed a persistent dry cough that he initially attributed to cold weather and his COPD. Over the following weeks the cough worsened and became productive. He began experiencing drenching night sweats, soaking through his undershirt two or three nights a week. He noticed his pants getting loose and his appetite declining. By mid-January he had lost approximately 15 pounds without trying.

On **January 22, 2026**, Robert presented to the Emergency Department at University of Illinois Hospital (UIH). He reported two months of worsening cough with occasional blood-streaked sputum, night sweats, fatigue, and unintentional weight loss. In the ED, his vital signs showed a low-grade temperature of 100.4°F, heart rate 92, blood pressure 142/88, respiratory rate 20, and SpO2 94% on room air. He appeared thin and fatigued.

A **chest X-ray** revealed a 3.5 cm cavitary lesion in the right upper lobe with surrounding infiltrate, along with mildly enlarged right hilar lymph nodes. Given the classic radiographic appearance, airborne isolation precautions were initiated immediately.

**Sputum AFB smears** (three specimens collected over the ED visit and first hospital day) were all positive for acid-fast bacilli on Ziehl-Neelsen stain. A GeneXpert MTB/RIF assay confirmed *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* complex without rifampin resistance detected.

Robert was **admitted to the hospital** on January 22 and placed in a negative-pressure isolation room. The admitting team initiated standard four-drug RIPE therapy:
- **Rifampin** 600 mg daily
- **Isoniazid** 300 mg daily (with pyridoxine 50 mg daily for neuropathy prophylaxis)
- **Isoniazid** was chosen despite his diabetes, with close monitoring of hepatic function
- **Pyrazinamide** 1500 mg daily
- **Ethambutol** 1200 mg daily

Additional workup during the admission included:
- **Complete blood count (CBC):** mild normocytic anemia (Hgb 11.2 g/dL), WBC 9.8 with normal differential
- **Comprehensive metabolic panel (CMP):** glucose 188, creatinine 1.1, AST 28, ALT 32, albumin 3.0 (mildly low)
- **HbA1c:** 8.1% (worsened from baseline, likely related to illness)
- **HIV screening:** negative (4th generation Ag/Ab combo)
- **Hepatitis B surface antigen:** negative
- **Hepatitis C antibody:** negative
- **Sputum culture:** sent to mycobacteriology lab (final culture results returned positive for *M. tuberculosis* at approximately 3 weeks; drug susceptibility testing showed susceptibility to all first-line agents — rifampin, isoniazid, pyrazinamide, ethambutol, streptomycin)

Robert tolerated the initiation of RIPE therapy. His liver enzymes were monitored every 48 hours during admission and remained stable. He experienced mild nausea for the first two days, managed with ondansetron. His cough began to improve. By hospital day 5, he was afebrile for over 48 hours with improving symptoms.

He was **discharged on January 27, 2026** (hospital day 5) to home with arrangements for **directly observed therapy (DOT)** through the UI Health Ambulatory TB Clinic. The Chicago Department of Public Health (CDPH) was notified per Illinois mandatory reporting requirements on January 23, and a **contact investigation** was initiated by the CDPH TB program. Robert provided a list of close contacts, which included his brother, a neighbor, and fellow church members.

The public health notification triggered the Permission Ticket access scenario: CDPH needs access to Robert's TB-related clinical data — diagnostic results, treatment records, and lab monitoring — without requiring access to his broader chronic disease history.

### Outpatient DOT and Follow-Up (February–April 2026)

After discharge, Robert began attending the **UI Health Ambulatory TB Clinic** for DOT visits and ongoing monitoring. His outpatient course proceeded as follows:

**Visit 1 — February 5, 2026 (2-week follow-up):** Robert was tolerating RIPE therapy. He reported mild GI upset and orange discoloration of urine (expected with rifampin). Weight was stable at 162 lbs (down from 177 lbs pre-illness). Vitals within acceptable range. Baseline visual acuity documented for ethambutol monitoring: 20/25 bilaterally. Repeat sputum AFB smear collected — still smear-positive but fewer organisms seen. Liver function tests (AST 35, ALT 38) were mildly elevated but within acceptable limits. Fasting glucose 172.

**Visit 2 — March 5, 2026 (6-week follow-up):** Clinical improvement noted. Night sweats resolved. Cough much improved with only occasional sputum production. Weight up 3 lbs to 165. Repeat sputum AFB smear was negative. Repeat chest X-ray showed interval decrease in size of right upper lobe cavity. LFTs stable (AST 31, ALT 34). CBC showed improving anemia (Hgb 12.0). Uric acid 8.9 (mildly elevated, consistent with pyrazinamide). Robert's metformin dose was reviewed; A1c recheck planned for month 3.

**Visit 3 — March 19, 2026 (8-week follow-up):** Interim DOT visit. Brief clinical check. Vitals stable. Medication adherence confirmed via DOT log. Visual acuity unchanged. No peripheral neuropathy symptoms. Basic labs drawn (CMP, CBC). No concerns.

**Visit 4 — April 2, 2026 (10-week follow-up):** Two consecutive sputum cultures (from 6-week and 8-week visits) have returned negative — confirming sputum culture conversion. Chest X-ray showed continued improvement of the cavitary lesion. LFTs remain stable. HbA1c rechecked at 7.6% (improved from 8.1% at admission, likely due to weight recovery and resumed diet). Discussion about transitioning to continuation phase: plan to discontinue pyrazinamide and ethambutol after completing 2 months of intensive phase. Continue rifampin and isoniazid for the 4-month continuation phase. Robert expressed some fatigue with daily clinic visits and was transitioned to three-times-weekly DOT for the continuation phase.

**Visit 5 — April 16, 2026 (12-week follow-up, planned):** Expected to confirm tolerance of continuation-phase regimen, collect follow-up sputum culture, monitor LFTs and CBC. Visual acuity testing can be discontinued after ethambutol is stopped.

**Visit 6 — May 14, 2026 (planned, end of month 4):** Scheduled continuation-phase monitoring. Repeat sputum culture, chest X-ray, LFTs. Assess clinical status and plan for eventual treatment completion at 6 months.

## Demo Relevance

Robert Davis's clinical scenario is purpose-built to demonstrate **Use Case 3: Public Health Investigation** within the SMART Permission Tickets reference implementation. His data exercises the following access constraint dimensions:

### Scope Constraints
A public health investigator (e.g., Chicago Department of Public Health TB program) conducting a contact investigation does not need access to Robert's full medical record. The Permission Ticket should demonstrate **resource-type scoping** — granting access to:
- `Condition` (the TB diagnosis, not necessarily his full problem list)
- `DiagnosticReport` (AFB smears, cultures, susceptibilities, chest X-rays)
- `Observation` (lab results — LFTs, CBC, sputum results; possibly vitals relevant to clinical status)
- `MedicationRequest` (RIPE therapy medications — important for confirming treatment adherence)
- `Patient` (demographics for case reporting)
- `Encounter` (to establish timeline of care)

Robert's chronic conditions (hypertension, diabetes, COPD), their associated medications (lisinopril, metformin, albuterol), and years of primary care history should be **excluded** by a well-scoped Permission Ticket, demonstrating that scopes effectively filter out irrelevant clinical data.

### Period Constraints
The TB episode spans a well-defined window: **January 22, 2026 through approximately April 30, 2026** (the intensive phase and early continuation phase). This allows demonstration of **temporal filtering** — a Permission Ticket scoped to this period would include the ED visit, inpatient stay, and outpatient DOT visits while excluding any historical data from Robert's decades of chronic disease management. The dense concentration of ~8 encounters and hundreds of resources in a 3–4 month window makes period-based filtering highly testable.

### Organization / Jurisdiction Constraints
Both of Robert's treatment sites — University of Illinois Hospital and the UI Health Ambulatory TB Clinic — are within the **same health system (UI Health)** in the **same city (Chicago)** and **same state (Illinois)**. This simplifies the organizational dimension: a Permission Ticket can be scoped to the UI Health system or to Illinois jurisdiction. This scenario deliberately avoids cross-state complexity (which other patient personas can test) and instead focuses the demo on **period and scope constraints as the primary filtering axes**.

### What Makes Robert's Data Distinctive for Testing
1. **Reportable condition trigger:** TB is a legally reportable disease, providing a realistic justification for public health data access that differs from patient-directed or provider-directed access.
2. **Dense, time-bounded episode:** ~8 encounters generating ~750–850 total FHIR resources concentrated in 3–4 months, layered on top of a longer chronic disease history that should be filterable.
3. **Rich lab data:** AFB smears (serial), cultures with susceptibility results, CBC, CMP, LFTs, HbA1c, HIV/hepatitis screening — excellent for testing DiagnosticReport and Observation filtering.
4. **Medication management:** RIPE therapy initiation and phase transition provides concrete MedicationRequest resources to filter for.
5. **Single health system, two sites:** Tests that organization-level permissions work correctly across affiliated facilities sharing the same EHR.
6. **Clear separation of "need-to-know" vs. "out-of-scope" data:** The chronic disease data (HTN, DM, COPD) from prior years provides a natural control set that should be excluded by properly configured Permission Tickets.
