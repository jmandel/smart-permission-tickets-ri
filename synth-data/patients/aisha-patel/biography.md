## Clinical Arc

Aisha was born full-term at 39 weeks via uncomplicated vaginal delivery at WakeMed in Raleigh in March 2020, weighing 3.2 kg. Her newborn screening was normal and she passed her hearing screen bilaterally. Her early infancy was unremarkable aside from mild cradle cap that resolved spontaneously.

### The Peanut Anaphylaxis (Age 2 — Late 2022)

The first sign of serious allergic disease came at age two. In October 2022, at a family Diwali gathering, Aisha accidentally ingested a cookie containing peanut butter. Within minutes she developed diffuse urticaria, facial angioedema, and audible wheezing. Priya recognized the severity and called 911. Aisha was transported to WakeMed Children's Emergency Department, where she presented with anaphylaxis — tachycardic at 155 bpm, oxygen saturation 93% on room air, with stridor and diffuse wheals. She received intramuscular epinephrine 0.15 mg (EpiPen Jr), IV diphenhydramine, IV methylprednisolone, and continuous albuterol nebulization. She was observed for six hours in the ED with resolution of symptoms and discharged home with an epinephrine auto-injector prescription (EpiPen Jr 0.15 mg, two-pack), a 5-day course of oral prednisolone, and strict peanut avoidance instructions. Her tryptase level drawn in the ED was elevated at 18.4 ng/mL (normal <11.5), confirming the anaphylactic mechanism.

This event was the catalyst for Aisha's referral to pediatric allergy. Her pediatrician at Triangle Pediatrics, Dr. Megan Harlow, placed the referral to UNC Pediatric Allergy & Immunology the following week.

### Allergy Workup and Atopic March (Age 2–3)

In November 2022, Aisha was seen for her initial evaluation at UNC Pediatric Allergy & Immunology by Dr. Sunita Krishnamurthy. A comprehensive workup revealed:

- **Peanut allergy:** Peanut-specific IgE 48.2 kU/L (Class 5, very high), skin prick test with 12 mm wheal. Given the history of anaphylaxis and high specific IgE, oral food challenge was deferred. Diagnosis: IgE-mediated peanut allergy with history of anaphylaxis.
- **Tree nut allergy:** Cashew-specific IgE 22.7 kU/L (Class 4), almond-specific IgE 8.3 kU/L (Class 3), walnut-specific IgE 15.1 kU/L (Class 3). Skin prick testing positive to cashew (9 mm wheal) and walnut (7 mm wheal). All tree nuts restricted given cross-reactivity risk and young age.
- **Total IgE:** 384 IU/mL (markedly elevated for age, normal <60 IU/mL for age 2–3).
- **Atopic dermatitis:** Moderate eczema on antecubital and popliteal fossae, noted since around 18 months. Started on triamcinolone 0.1% ointment for flares and daily ceramide-based moisturizer.
- **Allergic rhinitis:** Seasonal symptoms with nasal congestion and clear rhinorrhea. Started on cetirizine 2.5 mg daily.

Dr. Krishnamurthy discussed the atopic march — Aisha's progression from eczema to food allergy to allergic rhinitis was textbook — and counseled the family on the likelihood of asthma development.

### Asthma Diagnosis (Age 3 — 2023)

The prediction was accurate. Throughout winter 2022–2023, Aisha had three episodes of wheezing with viral upper respiratory infections. Her pediatrician Dr. Harlow initially managed these with PRN albuterol (ProAir HFA 90 mcg, 2 puffs every 4–6 hours via spacer with mask). After the third episode in February 2023, which required a short course of oral prednisolone, Dr. Harlow diagnosed **persistent asthma** and started Aisha on low-dose inhaled corticosteroid therapy: fluticasone propionate 44 mcg (Flovent HFA), 1 puff twice daily via spacer.

At Aisha's 3-year well-child visit in March 2023, Dr. Harlow documented the asthma action plan, reviewed spacer technique with Priya, and ensured the school had appropriate paperwork for albuterol administration. Growth was tracking normally at the 55th percentile for height and 48th percentile for weight. Developmental milestones were on track — Aisha was speaking in full sentences, running, climbing, and engaging in imaginative play.

### Escalation to Severe Persistent Asthma (Age 4–5 — 2024–2025)

Despite low-dose ICS, Aisha continued to have breakthrough symptoms, particularly with exercise, cold air exposure, and viral infections. At her 4-year well-child visit in March 2024, Dr. Harlow documented daytime symptoms 3–4 times per week and nighttime cough 2–3 times per month, classifying her asthma as **moderate persistent**. Therapy was stepped up to fluticasone/salmeterol (Advair HFA 45/21), 1 puff twice daily, and montelukast (Singulair) 4 mg chewable tablet daily was added.

In a follow-up visit with Dr. Krishnamurthy at UNC in May 2024, spirometry was attempted but was technically inadequate due to Aisha's age and cooperation. Fractional exhaled nitric oxide (FeNO) was 38 ppb (elevated, suggesting eosinophilic airway inflammation). Environmental control measures were reinforced — HEPA filtration, dust mite encasings, and avoidance of known triggers.

By early 2025, Aisha's asthma had progressed further despite dual-controller therapy. She was using albuterol rescue inhaler 3–4 times per week, had missed several days of school due to coughing and wheezing, and experienced a significant nighttime exacerbation every 2–3 weeks.

### The Severe Exacerbation (Age 5 — January 2025)

In January 2025, during a particularly bad cold, Aisha developed severe respiratory distress. Priya administered albuterol at home per the action plan, but after three treatments over 90 minutes without improvement, she drove Aisha to WakeMed Children's Emergency Department. Aisha presented with respiratory rate 38, heart rate 142, oxygen saturation 89% on room air, and diffuse expiratory wheezing with accessory muscle use. She received continuous albuterol nebulization, ipratropium bromide, IV methylprednisolone 1 mg/kg, and supplemental oxygen via nasal cannula. After four hours, she had improved sufficiently to avoid admission — oxygen saturation 96% on room air, respiratory rate 22, and decreased wheezing. She was discharged with a 5-day burst of oral prednisolone 1 mg/kg/day, continuation of her controller medications, and instructions for close pediatrician follow-up within 48 hours.

Dr. Harlow saw Aisha two days post-ED visit and reclassified her asthma as **severe persistent**. She increased the fluticasone/salmeterol to the 115/21 strength, continued montelukast, and referred her back to Dr. Krishnamurthy for reassessment and consideration of biologic therapy.

### Current Status (Age 6 — Early 2026)

At her most recent allergist visit in February 2026, Dr. Krishnamurthy reviewed Aisha's asthma control. On the higher-dose combination inhaler plus montelukast, her symptoms had improved modestly — rescue inhaler use was down to about twice weekly, and she had not had another ED visit. FeNO had come down to 24 ppb. Dr. Krishnamurthy discussed the possibility of omalizumab (Xolair) if control does not further improve, and also broached the topic of peanut oral immunotherapy, which Priya and Raj are considering but have not yet committed to. Repeat peanut-specific IgE was 41.6 kU/L (still very high), so a supervised oral food challenge is not yet appropriate.

Aisha's 6-year well-child visit at Triangle Pediatrics in March 2026 was normal. Growth remained appropriate — height 116 cm (60th percentile), weight 21 kg (55th percentile), BMI 15.6 (healthy). Vision screening was 20/25 bilaterally. She received her annual influenza vaccine and was up to date on all CDC-recommended immunizations. Dr. Harlow noted that Aisha is thriving academically and socially, with an active 504 plan at school accommodating her asthma and food allergies.

## Demo Relevance

### Use Case 2: Authorized Representative Access

This patient is the primary demonstration case for **UC2 — Authorized Representative** in the SMART Permission Tickets reference implementation. The key dynamics:

- **Subject:** Aisha Patel (minor child, age 6)
- **Requester:** Priya Patel (mother, authorized representative)
- **RelatedPerson relationship:** Priya → Aisha, relationship code `MTH` (mother)
- **Authorization basis:** Parental authority over minor child under North Carolina law
- **Verification:** Each site has verified Priya's identity and parental relationship at registration; the Permission Ticket includes a `verifiedAt` timestamp for each site
- **Subject resolution by identifier:** Aisha is known by different MRNs at each site (TP-2020-04182, UNC-7831924, WM-00293847), so the permission ticket must resolve the subject across organizations using identifiers

### Permission Ticket Constraint Dimensions Demonstrated

**1. Scopes (Resource Type Filtering)**
Aisha's data is rich across multiple FHIR resource types, making scope-based filtering highly demonstrable:
- **Immunization-only scope:** Triangle Pediatrics holds a complete childhood immunization series — a permission ticket scoped to `Immunization.read` would return just the vaccine records, which is a common real-world use case (e.g., school enrollment verification, state immunization registry query).
- **AllergyIntolerance scope:** UNC Allergy holds the richest allergy data — a scope limited to `AllergyIntolerance.read` would isolate the peanut and tree nut allergy records with their detailed reaction histories and IgE evidence.
- **MedicationRequest scope:** Filtering to just medications would surface the asthma controller regimen and epinephrine prescriptions across sites.
- **Observation scope:** Would return a large volume of vitals, lab values (IgE panels, tryptase), FeNO measurements, and growth chart data points.
- **Condition scope:** Would return the accumulated problem list across all three sites.

**2. Organizations (Cross-System Access)**
Three distinct health systems in the same metropolitan area each hold different facets of Aisha's care:
- **Triangle Pediatrics (NPI 1497823156):** Primary care, vaccines, longitudinal growth data
- **UNC Health (NPI 1356482079):** Subspecialty allergy and asthma management, detailed diagnostic testing
- **WakeMed (NPI 1238574960):** Acute emergency care, high-acuity episodic data

A permission ticket can be constrained to a single organization (e.g., "only release Triangle Pediatrics data") or can span all three. This demonstrates how a parent managing a child's care across multiple systems can grant or restrict access at the organizational level.

**3. Periods (Temporal Filtering)**
Aisha's clinical arc has clear temporal phases that make period-based constraints meaningful:
- **Pre-allergy diagnosis (2020–2022):** Routine well-child care only
- **Allergy workup and early asthma (2022–2024):** Diagnosis establishment, initial treatment
- **Severe asthma escalation (2024–2026):** Therapy intensification, ED visit, biologic discussion

A period constraint like "only records from 2024 onward" would exclude the initial allergy workup but include the asthma escalation and most recent management decisions.

**4. Jurisdictions**
All three sites are in North Carolina, so this patient demonstrates a **single-jurisdiction** scenario. The jurisdiction constraint (`NC`) applied to a permission ticket would include all of Aisha's data. This contrasts with multi-state patients in other scenarios and validates that jurisdiction filtering works correctly when all data falls within one state. North Carolina's laws regarding parental access to minor health records are straightforward for a 6-year-old (no special adolescent confidentiality provisions apply), making this a clean test case for representative authorization.

### Cross-Scenario Value

Aisha's immunization records are also relevant for **UC1 (direct patient/representative access)** demonstrations, as immunization record portability is a flagship use case for SMART Permission Tickets. Her case can show how a parent requests just their child's vaccine records from a pediatrician to share with a school or summer camp — a narrow scope + single organization permission ticket.
