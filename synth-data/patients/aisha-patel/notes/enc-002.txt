REASON FOR CONSULTATION:
Initial comprehensive allergy evaluation following peanut anaphylaxis, October 2022.

SUBJECTIVE:
Aisha is a 2-year-8-month-old Indian-American female referred by Dr. Harlow at Triangle Pediatrics for allergy evaluation after a peanut anaphylaxis event in October 2022. Her mother Priya provided a detailed history and brought the WakeMed ED discharge summary and tryptase result.

History of presenting illness: At a family gathering in October 2022, Aisha accidentally ingested a cookie containing peanut butter. Within minutes she developed diffuse urticaria, facial angioedema, and audible wheezing. She was transported to WakeMed Children's ED where she was found to be tachycardic at 155 bpm with SpO2 93% on room air, stridor, and diffuse wheals. She received IM epinephrine 0.15 mg, IV diphenhydramine, IV methylprednisolone, and continuous albuterol nebulization. Tryptase drawn in the ED was elevated at 18.4 ng/mL (normal <11.5), confirming anaphylactic mechanism. She was observed for 6 hours, symptoms resolved, and she was discharged with an EpiPen Jr prescription, a 5-day course of oral prednisolone, and strict peanut avoidance instructions.

Additional history: Mother reports onset of eczema at approximately 18 months of age, affecting the arm and knee creases. Seasonal nasal congestion and clear rhinorrhea noted over the past several months. No history of wheezing episodes to date. Aisha tolerates baked egg products (cake, muffins) without reaction. She has not knowingly ingested tree nuts. No known drug allergies.

Family history: Parental history not contributory for anaphylaxis. Detailed atopic family history was reviewed.

OBJECTIVE:
Vitals: HR 110 bpm, RR 24, BP 88/54 mmHg, SpO2 99% on room air, Temp 36.9C, Weight 12.8 kg (38th percentile), Height 89 cm (45th percentile).

Physical Examination:
General: Well-appearing, active toddler in no acute distress.
Skin: Moderate eczema with erythematous, lichenified patches on bilateral antecubital and popliteal fossae. Dry skin diffusely. No urticaria or angioedema.
HEENT: Clear nasal discharge bilaterally. Oropharynx clear. No conjunctival injection.
Lungs: Clear to auscultation bilaterally. No wheezing, crackles, or stridor.
Cardiovascular: Regular rate and rhythm, no murmurs.

ASSESSMENT:
1. IgE-mediated peanut allergy with history of anaphylaxis — peanut-specific IgE 48.2 kU/L with concordant clinical history of anaphylaxis in October 2022. Strongly positive SPT at 12 mm wheal. Oral food challenge deferred given high specific IgE and serious anaphylaxis history. Strict avoidance required.
2. Tree nut allergy (cashew, walnut, almond) — positive SPT and elevated specific IgE to cashew (22.7 kU/L), walnut (15.1 kU/L), and almond (8.3 kU/L). No known ingestion reaction to date. All tree nuts restricted given cross-reactivity risk and young age.
3. Atopic dermatitis, moderate — confirmed on exam with lichenified eczematous patches in flexural distribution. Onset approximately 18 months per parental history.
4. Allergic rhinitis — seasonal congestion and rhinorrhea. Nasal examination consistent with allergic etiology.
5. Elevated total IgE (384 IU/mL) — consistent with strong atopic phenotype.

Regarding egg sensitization: SPT was borderline at 3 mm (histamine control 4 mm) and specific IgE was low positive at 1.8 kU/L. Patient has been tolerating baked egg products without reaction. Advised continued inclusion of baked egg in the diet. A supervised oral egg challenge could be considered in the future if desired but is not clinically urgent at this time.

I discussed the concept of the atopic march with the family. Aisha's progression from eczema to food allergy to allergic rhinitis is characteristic of a strong atopic phenotype, and given her markedly elevated total IgE, the development of asthma is likely. Advised vigilance for recurrent wheeze or cough, particularly with viral illness or exercise.

PLAN:
1. Strict avoidance of peanut and all tree nuts (cashew, walnut, almond, and other tree nuts given cross-reactivity risk).
2. EpiPen Jr (epinephrine 0.15 mg auto-injector) two-pack — prescription confirmed. Reviewed indications for use, injection technique, and storage with both parents.
3. Anaphylaxis emergency action plan created and signed — copies provided for family, pediatrician (Dr. Harlow), and daycare.
4. Triamcinolone 0.1% ointment — apply to eczema flares on affected areas twice daily as needed.
5. Ceramide-based moisturizer (CeraVe) — apply to affected areas daily for skin barrier maintenance.
6. Cetirizine 2.5 mg oral solution — once daily for allergic rhinitis.
7. Continue baked egg in diet as tolerated.
8. Follow-up in 10-12 months for repeat specific IgE monitoring and clinical reassessment. Return sooner if asthma symptoms (recurrent wheeze, persistent cough) develop.
9. Consultation note and food allergy management plan sent to Dr. Harlow at Triangle Pediatrics.
