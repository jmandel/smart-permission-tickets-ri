# Carlos Medina — Patient Biography

## Patient Overview

Carlos Roberto Medina is a 51-year-old Hispanic male born on August 14, 1974, in San Antonio, Texas. He is a lifelong Texan who grew up on the south side of San Antonio, the son of a machinist and a school secretary. Carlos attended local public schools, played varsity baseball at Harlandale High School, and completed his education degree at Texas A&M–San Antonio. He has taught high school shop (industrial arts) for 26 years and has coached JV and varsity baseball for the last 18. He is married to Rosa Elena Medina (née Garza), a dental hygienist. They have three children: the eldest two are out of the house, and their youngest daughter, Sofia (age 16), is a junior at the same high school where Carlos teaches.

## Clinical Arc

### Before the Arthritis (2015–2020)

Carlos established care at Alamo Family Practice in 2015 when the clinic opened near his home, switching from an older practice that had closed. His early years there were unremarkable: annual physicals, a flu shot each fall, occasional acute visits for a sinus infection or a coaching-related muscle strain. He carried mild diagnoses — borderline overweight (BMI hovering around 27–28), mild seasonal allergies managed with cetirizine, and occasional low back pain from years of demonstrating woodworking techniques and throwing batting practice. His blood pressure ran slightly high-normal (128–132/82–86), and his PCP, Dr. Linda Tran, counseled him on diet and exercise but held off on antihypertensives.

He had no significant surgical history, no chronic disease burden, and no family history of autoimmune conditions — though his mother had osteoarthritis in her knees. His immunizations were up to date: Tdap booster in 2017, annual influenza, and he received his COVID-19 primary series (Pfizer) in early 2021 plus a booster later that year.

**Allergies**: Carlos has a documented allergy to sulfonamide antibiotics (developed a diffuse maculopapular rash after a course of trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole in 2003). No known food or environmental allergies beyond seasonal rhinitis.

### The Onset (Late 2020–Early 2021)

In the fall of 2020, Carlos noticed stiffness in his fingers in the mornings. He attributed it to the cooler weather and to gripping tools in the shop all day. By December, he was having trouble making a full fist in the mornings, and the stiffness lasted an hour or more. Both hands were affected symmetrically — the metacarpophalangeal and proximal interphalangeal joints were swollen and tender. His feet ached after standing through a full day of teaching, and he noticed puffiness around his wrists.

He mentioned the symptoms to Rosa, who urged him to see Dr. Tran. He put it off until February 2021, when the stiffness was interfering with his ability to demonstrate lathe work and grip a bat during early-season practice. At his visit on February 8, 2021, Dr. Tran noted bilateral MCP and PIP joint swelling with warmth, limited wrist extension bilaterally, and tenderness at the second and third MTP joints of both feet. She ordered labs: RF came back at 78 IU/mL (normal <14), anti-CCP was strongly positive at >250 U/mL, ESR was 42 mm/hr, and CRP was 2.8 mg/dL. CBC was unremarkable. Basic metabolic panel was normal. Liver enzymes were normal at baseline (ALT 24, AST 22).

Dr. Tran told Carlos she was concerned about rheumatoid arthritis and referred him urgently to a rheumatologist.

### Diagnosis and Methotrexate (Spring–Fall 2021)

Carlos was seen at South Texas Rheumatology on March 15, 2021, by Dr. Alejandro Fuentes. Dr. Fuentes performed a detailed joint exam, confirming symmetric polyarthritis involving the hands (MCPs, PIPs bilaterally), wrists, and feet (MTPs). He ordered bilateral hand and foot X-rays, which showed periarticular osteopenia and early marginal erosions at the second and third MCP joints of the right hand. The radiologist's read confirmed findings consistent with early erosive rheumatoid arthritis.

Dr. Fuentes diagnosed Carlos with seropositive rheumatoid arthritis (ICD-10 M05.79) based on the 2010 ACR/EULAR criteria: he met criteria on joint involvement (large and small joints, symmetric), serology (high-titer RF and anti-CCP), acute-phase reactants (elevated ESR and CRP), and duration (symptoms present for >6 weeks). His baseline DAS28-CRP was calculated at 5.1, indicating high disease activity. Functional assessment showed a HAQ-DI of 1.2 — moderate functional impairment, primarily in gripping and fine motor tasks.

Carlos was started on methotrexate 15 mg weekly (oral) with folic acid 1 mg daily. Dr. Fuentes counseled him on the medication, ordered baseline hepatitis B and C serologies (both negative), a chest X-ray (clear), and scheduled follow-up labs in 4 weeks. Carlos was also prescribed naproxen 500 mg twice daily as a bridge for symptom control.

At his 4-week lab check (April 2021), CBC and LFTs were normal. At his 3-month follow-up (June 2021), Carlos reported improvement — less morning stiffness (down to about 30 minutes), reduced swelling — but his DAS28-CRP was still 4.2, indicating moderate disease activity. Dr. Fuentes increased the methotrexate to 20 mg weekly and continued monitoring.

### Methotrexate Toxicity (Fall 2021)

Carlos tolerated the higher dose initially. At his September 2021 lab check, liver enzymes were mildly elevated (ALT 52, AST 48) but within the range where Dr. Fuentes opted to continue with close monitoring. By November 2021 — six months on the 20 mg dose — his labs showed ALT 98 and AST 82, more than twice the upper limit of normal. Carlos also reported some nausea and fatigue.

Dr. Fuentes immediately discontinued methotrexate due to hepatotoxicity. He ordered a hepatic panel to rule out other causes (normal bilirubin, albumin, and alkaline phosphatase; hepatitis panel remained negative). He advised Carlos to stop alcohol entirely (Carlos was a light social drinker — a few beers on weekends) and scheduled a follow-up in 4 weeks to confirm enzyme normalization before starting an alternative DMARD.

At the December 2021 follow-up, ALT had come down to 38 and AST to 34. Carlos's joint symptoms had flared in the interim without DMARD therapy — his DAS28-CRP climbed back to 4.9.

### Leflunomide Trial (2022)

In January 2022, Dr. Fuentes started Carlos on leflunomide 20 mg daily as the second-line conventional DMARD. He explained that they needed to try another conventional agent before moving to biologics, both for clinical appropriateness and because the insurance plan required step therapy documentation.

Over the first three months, Carlos had modest improvement. His DAS28-CRP came down to 4.4 by April 2022. Liver enzymes remained stable (ALT 28, AST 26). But his grip strength, measured by dynamometer, was declining: 22 kg right hand, 18 kg left hand (down from a pre-disease baseline Dr. Fuentes estimated at 40+ kg for a man who works with his hands daily).

By July 2022, after six months on leflunomide, Carlos was frustrated. His DAS28-CRP was 4.8 — moderate-to-high disease activity. Morning stiffness had crept back up to 45 minutes. He was having difficulty demonstrating techniques in the shop and had to hand off batting practice to his assistant coach because his wrists couldn't take it. His HAQ-DI had worsened to 1.5.

Dr. Fuentes ordered follow-up bilateral hand X-rays in August 2022. Compared to the March 2021 baseline, there were new erosions at the second MCP of the left hand and worsening of the right-hand erosions, along with joint space narrowing at bilateral wrists. The radiologist noted "interval progression of erosive disease."

Dr. Fuentes documented leflunomide as an inadequate response: six months of therapy at full dose with persistent moderate-to-high disease activity, worsening functional status, and radiographic progression.

### The Biologic Discussion and Prior Authorization (Late 2022–Present)

At a visit in September 2022, Dr. Fuentes discussed escalation to biologic therapy with Carlos. He recommended adalimumab — specifically Hadlima (adalimumab-bwwd), a biosimilar, which the Aetna formulary preferred. The plan was subcutaneous injection 40 mg every two weeks, continuing leflunomide as background therapy initially.

Before starting, Dr. Fuentes's office needed to obtain prior authorization from Aetna. The prior auth submission required:

- **Diagnosis confirmation**: Seropositive RA (M05.79) with supporting serology (RF 78, anti-CCP >250)
- **Failed conventional DMARDs**: Methotrexate (discontinued for hepatotoxicity — documented ALT 98, AST 82) and leflunomide (inadequate response — DAS28-CRP 4.8 after 6 months at full dose)
- **Disease activity scores**: Serial DAS28-CRP values (5.1 → 4.2 → 4.9 → 4.4 → 4.8) demonstrating persistent moderate-to-high activity
- **Functional status**: HAQ-DI scores (1.2 → 1.5) showing decline
- **Imaging**: Baseline and follow-up hand X-rays showing erosive progression
- **Current labs**: Most recent CRP (2.4 mg/dL), ESR (38 mm/hr), CBC, LFTs

This is the point at which Aetna, as the payer, uses a Permission Ticket to request access to Carlos's clinical records at both South Texas Rheumatology and Alamo Family Practice to adjudicate the prior authorization.

Carlos continues to be seen regularly. His PCP, Dr. Tran, manages his general health and coordinates on lab monitoring. He sees Dr. Fuentes every 2–3 months for RA disease activity assessment. His current medications are leflunomide 20 mg daily, folic acid 1 mg daily (continued from the methotrexate era as a habit), naproxen 500 mg as needed (he uses it most days), cetirizine 10 mg daily for seasonal allergies, and omeprazole 20 mg daily (started in mid-2022 for NSAID gastroprotection after he reported some epigastric discomfort).

## Demo Relevance

### Use Case Alignment

Carlos's scenario maps to **UC5: Payer Claims Adjudication — Prior Authorization Variant**. Aetna, as the payer organization, requests access to Carlos's records to adjudicate a prior authorization for biologic therapy (adalimumab biosimilar, Hadlima). This is a step-therapy prior auth, meaning the payer must verify that Carlos tried and failed cheaper conventional DMARDs before the expensive biologic can be approved.

### Permission Ticket Constraint Dimensions Exercised

**Scopes (Resource-type constraints)**:
The payer's Permission Ticket does not need Carlos's entire medical record — it needs specific resource types relevant to the prior auth:
- `Condition` — RA diagnosis (M05.79), seropositive status
- `Observation` — Disease activity scores (DAS28-CRP values over time), inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR), liver enzymes (documenting methotrexate toxicity), RF and anti-CCP titers
- `DiagnosticReport` — Hand and foot X-ray reports showing baseline erosions and interval progression
- `MedicationRequest` — Full DMARD prescribing history (methotrexate 15mg→20mg, leflunomide 20mg, pending adalimumab), with start/stop dates and reasons for discontinuation
- `Procedure` — Joint imaging procedures, disease activity assessments
- `DocumentReference` — Clinical notes from key visits (diagnosis, medication changes, prior auth letter)

This demonstrates that Permission Tickets can constrain access to specific FHIR resource types rather than granting blanket access.

**Periods (Temporal constraints)**:
The payer needs records from **2021–present** — the full RA treatment arc from initial diagnosis through the current prior auth request. Earlier records (Carlos's routine primary care from 2015–2020) are not relevant to the biologic authorization and should not be included. This exercises the period constraint dimension, showing how a Permission Ticket can limit data access to a clinically relevant time window.

**Organizations (Site constraints)**:
The Permission Ticket must authorize access at **two separate data-holding organizations**: Alamo Family Practice (NPI 1467582390) and South Texas Rheumatology (NPI 1923847561). The payer needs records from both because the PCP holds initial diagnostic labs and referral documentation, while the rheumatologist holds disease activity monitoring, DMARD management records, and imaging. This demonstrates cross-organization data gathering under a single authorization framework.

**Requester identity**:
The requester is an **Organization** (Aetna, NPI 1568473920), not an individual practitioner. This exercises the organizational requester pathway and demonstrates that Permission Tickets support payer-initiated access requests.

**Subject resolution**:
Carlos is a **known patient** at both data-holding sites — subject resolution is by reference (his MRN or patient resource ID at each site), not by demographic matching.

### What Makes This Scenario Distinctive

- **Step therapy documentation**: The payer needs to verify a specific sequence of treatment failures — methotrexate stopped for toxicity, leflunomide failed for inadequate response — before approving a biologic. This requires longitudinal medication records with reasons for changes.
- **Quantifiable disease activity**: DAS28-CRP scores are standardized, numeric measures that the payer can objectively evaluate. The series (5.1 → 4.2 → 4.9 → 4.4 → 4.8) tells a clear story of inadequate disease control.
- **Lab trends as evidence**: CRP and ESR trends document ongoing inflammation; liver enzyme elevation documents methotrexate toxicity. These are concrete data points the payer reviews, not subjective narratives.
- **Imaging progression**: Comparison of baseline and follow-up hand X-rays provides objective structural evidence that the disease is progressing despite conventional therapy.
- **Single jurisdiction**: Both sites are in Texas, so this scenario does not exercise the jurisdiction constraint. It focuses instead on scope and period constraints with an organizational requester — a complementary pattern to scenarios that test multi-state jurisdiction constraints.
- **Prior auth vs. claims**: This is a prospective authorization (before treatment starts), contrasting with retrospective claims adjudication scenarios. The Permission Ticket is issued proactively as part of the approval workflow.
