## Clinical Arc

Harold Washington spent thirty-one years as a letter carrier for the United States Postal Service in Jacksonville, Florida, retiring in 2008 at age 62. He and his wife Dorothy enjoyed an active retirement — church on Sundays at Bethel Baptist, morning walks along the St. Johns Riverwalk, and weekly dominoes with friends at the VFW hall.

Harold's health history was unremarkable until his mid-sixties. In 2015, at age 68, he established care with Dr. Cynthia Okafor at Riverside Internal Medicine in Jacksonville after his previous PCP retired. At that first visit, his blood pressure was 152/94 and his fasting glucose was 138 mg/dL. Dr. Okafor started him on lisinopril 10 mg daily for hypertension. Over the following months, lab work confirmed a hemoglobin A1c of 7.2%, and she diagnosed type 2 diabetes mellitus. She started metformin 500 mg twice daily and referred him to a diabetes educator. His lipid panel showed LDL of 158 mg/dL, and she added atorvastatin 20 mg daily for hyperlipidemia.

For the next several years, Harold was a compliant and stable patient. He came in for quarterly visits, then transitioned to every-four-to-six-month visits once his numbers improved. His A1c settled to 6.8%, blood pressure ran 130–138/78–84 on lisinopril (uptitrated to 20 mg in 2017), and his LDL came down to 98 mg/dL. He got his flu shot every fall, completed his pneumococcal series, and had a negative colonoscopy in 2016. Annual wellness visits included PHQ-2 screening (always 0/6), fall risk assessment, and a review of his home safety. His weight was steady around 198 lbs on a 5'10" frame (BMI ~28.4). He was on a low-sodium, low-glycemic diet and walked three to four times a week.

In early 2022, Dorothy noticed that Harold was repeating himself more than usual. He forgot their grandson's birthday party and twice left the stove on. She mentioned this to Dr. Okafor at Harold's March 2022 visit. Dr. Okafor administered a Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE) in the office; Harold scored 24/30, losing points on recall and orientation to date. She referred him to First Coast Neurology Associates to see Dr. Raymond Patel.

Dr. Patel saw Harold in April 2022. He performed a detailed neurological examination, ordered an MRI of the brain (which showed mild generalized cortical atrophy and hippocampal volume loss, no acute infarcts), and ran a comprehensive metabolic panel and thyroid studies (all normal, ruling out reversible causes). He administered the Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA); Harold scored 20/30. Based on the clinical picture — insidious onset of episodic memory impairment with corroborating hippocampal atrophy and no reversible etiology — Dr. Patel diagnosed Harold with Alzheimer's disease, early onset type, mild stage. He started donepezil 5 mg daily, titrated to 10 mg after four weeks.

Harold and Dorothy were shaken by the diagnosis, but they organized. In November 2022, at Margaret's urging, Harold signed a durable power of attorney for healthcare, naming Margaret as his agent. Dr. Patel saw Harold every three to four months through 2022 and 2023. His MMSE hovered around 22–23/30 — stable on donepezil. He could still manage his medications with Dorothy's reminders, drive short familiar routes, and participate in conversation. Dr. Okafor continued his chronic disease management in parallel; his diabetes and hypertension remained well controlled.

In June 2024, Dorothy was diagnosed with pancreatic adenocarcinoma, already metastatic. She declined rapidly and died on June 22, 2024. Harold was devastated. Without Dorothy's daily structure and reminders, his cognitive decline accelerated visibly over the summer. Margaret, who lived in Decatur, Georgia, began flying down to Jacksonville every other weekend. She found expired food in the refrigerator, unopened pill bottles, and unpaid bills. In September 2024, Harold was found by a neighbor wandering two blocks from home, confused about where he was going. The following week, he backed his car into a mailbox pulling out of the driveway.

Margaret made the decision to move Harold to Georgia. In October 2024, she arranged for him to leave Jacksonville. His last visit at Riverside Internal Medicine was October 3, 2024; Dr. Okafor provided a transfer-of-care summary. His last visit at First Coast Neurology was October 10, 2024; Dr. Patel documented his most recent MMSE at 19/30 (declining from 22) and wrote a referral letter recommending continued cholinesterase inhibitor therapy and re-evaluation in three months.

Harold arrived in Decatur in late October 2024 and moved into Margaret's home. Margaret established him at DeKalb Primary Care in November 2024 with Dr. Anita Desai. At his new-patient visit, Dr. Desai reviewed the transfer records from Jacksonville. She continued lisinopril 20 mg, metformin 500 mg BID, and atorvastatin 20 mg. His blood pressure was 142/86 (white-coat effect likely, given the move stress); she added amlodipine 5 mg daily to improve control. His A1c was 7.0% — slightly up from his baseline of 6.8%, attributed to medication non-adherence during the transition. She ordered baseline labs, a diabetic foot exam, and updated his immunizations (COVID booster, flu shot). She also performed a PHQ-2 (score: 3/6, positive screen) and referred him to a social worker for bereavement support and caregiver resources for Margaret.

In January 2025, Harold saw Dr. Lisa Nakamura at the Emory Cognitive Neurology Center in Atlanta for a comprehensive geriatric neurology evaluation. Dr. Nakamura performed a full neurocognitive battery. His MMSE was now 17/30 — a five-point drop from ten months earlier, crossing from mild to moderate Alzheimer's. His MoCA was 13/30. She noted increased difficulty with instrumental activities of daily living: he could no longer manage finances, cook safely, or be left alone for more than a few hours. She discontinued donepezil monotherapy and started the donepezil 10 mg / memantine 28 mg extended-release combination (Namzaric). She also discussed advance care planning with Margaret, documented Harold's preferences (comfort-focused care, no intubation), and placed a referral for occupational therapy to work on home safety modifications.

In February 2025, Harold fell in the bathroom at Margaret's home while getting up from the toilet at night. Margaret called 911. He was taken to the Emory Decatur Hospital emergency department. CT head was negative for acute intracranial hemorrhage or fracture. X-ray of the right hip was negative. He had a small forehead laceration closed with dermabond and a right wrist contusion. He was discharged home with fall precautions. Margaret subsequently had grab bars installed and removed throw rugs.

Harold returned to Dr. Desai in March 2025 for a follow-up. Blood pressure was improved at 132/78 on the two-drug regimen. A1c had come back down to 6.9%. She counseled on fall prevention, reviewed his medication list to minimize sedating agents (he was not on any), and ordered a vitamin D level (which was low at 18 ng/mL — she started cholecalciferol 2000 IU daily). She also documented a functional status update: Harold needs assistance with bathing, dressing, and medication management; he is continent and ambulatory with a steady gait using no assistive device.

Harold saw Dr. Nakamura again in April 2025 for a three-month follow-up. His MMSE was 17/30 — stable since starting the combination therapy. She was cautiously optimistic about stabilization. She ordered a repeat MRI brain (showing progressive hippocampal atrophy but no new findings) and planned to see him again in three months.

## Demo Relevance

Harold Washington's record is purpose-built to exercise **Use Case 2: Authorized Representative** in the SMART Permission Tickets reference implementation. Here is how each access constraint dimension applies:

### Authorized Representative (UC2)

Margaret Washington-Clarke is Harold's daughter and healthcare power of attorney holder. She is represented in the system as a **RelatedPerson** resource (relationship code `DAU` — daughter) linked to Harold's Patient resource. When Margaret authenticates through a patient-facing app to request Harold's records, the permission ticket must capture:

- **Subject:** Harold Washington (resolved via MPI identifier across all four provider systems)
- **Requester:** Margaret Washington-Clarke as RelatedPerson
- **Details — basis:** Patient-designated (durable power of attorney for healthcare, executed 2022-11-15)
- **Details — verifiedAt:** Timestamp of the most recent verification of the POA document

This allows the system to distinguish Margaret's authorized representative access from Harold's own access (if he could authenticate) or from a third-party payer or researcher requesting the same data.

### Scopes

Harold's record supports demonstrating **scope-based filtering** at several levels:

- **Broad scopes (full clinical access):** Margaret, as authorized representative, would typically receive permission tickets with broad clinical scopes — she can see all of Harold's clinical data across categories (conditions, medications, observations, encounters, documents).
- **Narrow scopes (category-level filtering):** A researcher studying Alzheimer's progression might request only cognitive assessment data (MMSE, MoCA scores, neurology notes) — the system could issue a permission ticket scoped to `Observation?category=cognitive-status` and `DocumentReference` from neurology encounters only.
- **Sensitive data contrast:** Harold's cognitive decline data (Alzheimer's diagnosis, MMSE/MoCA scores, neurology notes) could be considered sensitive compared to his routine chronic disease data (blood pressure, A1c, lipid panels). This provides a good test for scope-based sensitivity policies.

### Periods

The timeline of Harold's care creates natural period boundaries for testing:

- **Florida period (2015–2024):** Nine years of primary care and 2.5 years of neurology in Jacksonville. A permission ticket constrained to `period=2015-01-01..2024-10-31` would return only Florida-era records.
- **Georgia period (2024–present):** New providers established after the October 2024 move. A permission ticket constrained to `period=2024-11-01..` would return only post-relocation records.
- **Overlap / transition:** October 2024 is a boundary month where the last Florida encounters and the first Georgia encounters coexist. This tests edge cases in period filtering.

### Jurisdictions

Harold's care spans **two U.S. states** with different healthcare privacy laws:

- **Florida (FL):** Riverside Internal Medicine and First Coast Neurology Associates. Florida follows federal HIPAA rules with some state-specific provisions around mental health and substance abuse records.
- **Georgia (GA):** DeKalb Primary Care, Emory Cognitive Neurology Center, and Emory Decatur Hospital ED. Georgia has its own consent and privacy statutes.

A permission ticket can be constrained by jurisdiction (e.g., `jurisdiction=FL` or `jurisdiction=GA`) to limit data release to records originating from providers in a specific state. This exercises cross-state data sharing scenarios that are common when patients relocate.

### Organizations

Four distinct provider organizations (plus the ED) allow testing of **organization-level access constraints**:

- Permission tickets can be scoped to a single organization (e.g., only Emory Cognitive Neurology Center for a neurology-specific research query).
- Permission tickets can span multiple organizations (e.g., all Georgia providers, or all providers) for comprehensive care access.
- Each organization has its own NPI, enabling unambiguous organizational scoping.
