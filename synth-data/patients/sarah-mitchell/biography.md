Sarah is a 32-year-old white woman who works as a freelance graphic designer. She lived in Portland, Oregon from 2016 through mid-2024, then relocated to Seattle, Washington for a fresh start after completing treatment for alcohol use disorder. She is single with no children and lives alone with her cat. She is generally health-literate, tech-savvy, and actively engaged in managing her own health records through a patient-facing app.

## Clinical Arc

Sarah's health story begins unremarkably. Through her teens and early twenties she was generally healthy — routine wellness visits, seasonal flu shots, the occasional urgent care visit for a sinus infection. She established care at Hawthorne Health Center in Portland in 2019 after aging off her mother's insurance plan and obtaining her own marketplace coverage.

### The Depression That Wasn't Just Depression (2019)

In early 2019, Sarah presented to her PCP, Dr. Emily Nakamura at Hawthorne Health Center, with complaints of persistent low mood, difficulty concentrating on freelance projects, poor appetite, and disrupted sleep lasting about six weeks. She scored a 17 on the PHQ-9 (moderately severe depression). Dr. Nakamura diagnosed major depressive disorder and started her on sertraline 50mg daily. Sarah tolerated the medication and was titrated up to 100mg over the next two months.

By late spring 2019, something shifted. Sarah reported feeling "amazing" — she was sleeping only four or five hours a night but felt energized, took on an ambitious number of freelance clients, and spent impulsively on design equipment she didn't need. She didn't recognize this as a problem; she told Dr. Nakamura she felt the sertraline was finally working. Dr. Nakamura, noting the mood shift and sleep changes, referred Sarah to psychiatry for further evaluation.

### Bipolar II Diagnosis (2019)

Sarah saw Dr. James Okafor at Portland Behavioral Health Associates in July 2019. Over a thorough initial psychiatric evaluation — lasting 90 minutes — Dr. Okafor took a detailed mood history and identified a pattern of hypomanic episodes dating back to Sarah's college years: periods of decreased sleep need, increased goal-directed activity, pressured speech, and impulsive spending, each lasting four to seven days, interspersed with longer depressive episodes. The sertraline had tipped the balance, triggering a more pronounced hypomanic phase.

Dr. Okafor diagnosed bipolar II disorder and tapered the sertraline over four weeks while cross-titrating to lamotrigine, starting at 25mg and slowly increasing to the target dose of 200mg daily over about eight weeks (following the standard slow titration to minimize risk of Stevens-Johnson syndrome). By fall 2019, Sarah was stable on lamotrigine 200mg. Her mood evened out. She felt less creative during the transition — she missed the hypomanic productivity — but acknowledged the depressive crashes were devastating.

Through 2020 and 2021, Sarah followed up with Dr. Okafor roughly quarterly. Her lamotrigine level was checked periodically (typically 4–8 mcg/mL, therapeutic). She had a mild depressive episode in winter 2020 during the early COVID-19 pandemic — she was isolated, freelance work dried up, and she struggled with motivation. Dr. Okafor added quetiapine 50mg at bedtime to help with insomnia and mood stabilization. This combination — lamotrigine 200mg daily plus quetiapine 50mg nightly — became her stable regimen. Basic metabolic panels and CBC were checked annually; her labs remained unremarkable aside from mildly elevated triglycerides (178 mg/dL) likely attributable to quetiapine, which Dr. Okafor monitored.

At Hawthorne Health Center, her PCP continued to manage routine primary care: annual wellness exams with standard vitals (BP typically 118/74, HR 72, BMI 24.1), a Pap smear in 2021 (normal), seasonal flu vaccinations, and a COVID-19 vaccine series (Pfizer, primary series in spring 2021, booster in late 2021).

### Alcohol Use Disorder (2022–2023)

In 2022, Sarah's freelance business hit a rough patch. A major client pulled a contract, and financial stress compounded a depressive episode. She began drinking wine most evenings to "take the edge off." By fall 2022, she was drinking a bottle of wine nightly, sometimes more on weekends. She missed a psychiatry appointment. When she did see Dr. Okafor in November 2022, she minimized her drinking, but he noted she seemed more withdrawn and her PHQ-9 had climbed to 14.

By January 2023, Sarah recognized the problem herself. She was drinking daily, had tried unsuccessfully to cut back several times, and was experiencing mild withdrawal symptoms (tremor, anxiety, sweating) when she skipped a day. She researched outpatient programs and self-referred to Bridgetown Recovery Center, an addiction medicine clinic in Portland.

At Bridgetown, Sarah was evaluated by Dr. Rosa Delgado, an addiction medicine specialist. Her AUDIT score was 28 (high risk). She was diagnosed with alcohol use disorder, moderate severity (DSM-5, meeting 5 of 11 criteria: drinking more than intended, persistent desire to cut down, craving, continued use despite interpersonal problems, tolerance). Her liver enzymes were mildly elevated (AST 52, ALT 61, GGT 89) but hepatic ultrasound was unremarkable. Dr. Delgado started her on naltrexone 50mg daily to reduce cravings and enrolled her in a 12-week intensive outpatient program (IOP) — three group sessions per week plus individual counseling.

Sarah's IOP ran from February through April 2023. She achieved sobriety in mid-April 2023. Her liver enzymes normalized within two months. She began attending AA meetings twice weekly. She had a follow-up visit with Dr. Delgado in July 2023 to confirm stability and transition to ongoing aftercare, with a plan to continue naltrexone indefinitely. Dr. Delgado coordinated with Dr. Okafor by phone (documented in both charts) regarding the interaction between Sarah's bipolar management and her recovery.

### The Move to Seattle (2024)

By early 2024, Sarah had been sober for nearly a year and felt ready for a change. Portland held too many associations with her drinking period — the bars she used to frequent, the social circles that enabled it. She moved to Seattle in June 2024, settling in the Ballard neighborhood.

Sarah established with a new PCP, Dr. Kevin Tran at Ballard Family Medicine, in July 2024. Her new patient visit included a comprehensive history, medication reconciliation (lamotrigine 200mg, quetiapine 50mg, naltrexone 50mg), vitals (BP 116/72, HR 68, weight 142 lbs, height 5'6", BMI 22.9), and routine labs (CBC normal, CMP normal, lipid panel showing triglycerides 162 mg/dL, LDL 108 mg/dL). Dr. Tran noted her bipolar II and alcohol use disorder in sustained remission and set up a care plan for ongoing monitoring. She had a follow-up in October 2024 for a routine check and flu shot, and another visit in early 2025 for annual wellness.

She also established with Dr. Anita Patel at Puget Sound Psychiatry in August 2024. Dr. Patel conducted a new patient psychiatric evaluation, reviewed Sarah's Portland records, and continued the current medication regimen without changes. Sarah's mood was stable — she described feeling "the most even I've been in years." Dr. Patel scheduled quarterly follow-ups. A second visit in November 2024 confirmed continued stability; lamotrigine level was therapeutic at 5.8 mcg/mL. A third visit in February 2025 was similarly unremarkable.

### Current Status (Early 2025)

Sarah is doing well. Her bipolar II disorder is well-controlled on lamotrigine 200mg daily and quetiapine 50mg nightly. Her alcohol use disorder has been in sustained remission for nearly two years; she continues naltrexone 50mg daily and attends AA meetings weekly. She has no active medical problems aside from her managed psychiatric conditions. She is up to date on immunizations. She has no known drug allergies. She has a documented allergy to sulfa antibiotics (trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole), which caused a rash in childhood — this is noted in all five provider charts.

## Demo Relevance

Sarah Mitchell's record is purpose-built to exercise the core constraint dimensions of the SMART Permission Tickets specification in a Use Case 1 (patient access) scenario:

### Scopes (Data Category Filtering)
Sarah's health data naturally partitions into three sensitivity tiers:
- **General medical data** (Hawthorne Health Center, Ballard Family Medicine): routine vitals, preventive care, labs, immunizations. Accessible with standard clinical scopes.
- **Mental health data** (Portland Behavioral Health Associates, Puget Sound Psychiatry): psychiatric evaluations, bipolar II diagnosis and management, mood stabilizer prescriptions, PHQ-9 scores. Carries mental health sensitivity security labels.
- **Substance use disorder data** (Bridgetown Recovery Center): AUD diagnosis, IOP treatment records, naltrexone initiation, AUDIT scores, urine drug screens. Carries 42 CFR Part 2 security labels requiring explicit consent.

A Permission Ticket can be constrained to specific scopes — for example, granting access only to general medical observations, or only to mental health conditions and medications, or explicitly including (or excluding) 42 CFR Part 2 protected data. Sarah's medication list is particularly illustrative: lamotrigine and quetiapine are mental health medications, naltrexone is an SUD medication, and a flu vaccine is general medical — all on the same patient, requiring scope-based segmentation.

### Organizations (Multi-Provider Filtering)
Five distinct provider organizations across two care eras (Portland 2019–2024, Seattle 2024–present). Permission Tickets can be constrained to specific organizations by NPI or organization reference, allowing access to, say, only Ballard Family Medicine and Puget Sound Psychiatry (the Seattle providers), or only Bridgetown Recovery Center (if the patient has consented to share SUD records with a specific party).

### Periods (Temporal Filtering)
The clear temporal break in mid-2024 (Portland → Seattle move) creates a natural period boundary. Permission Tickets can constrain access to specific date ranges — for example, only records from 2024 onward (Seattle era), or only records from 2023 (capturing the SUD treatment period), or the full longitudinal record from 2019 to present.

### Jurisdictions (State-Level Filtering)
Three Oregon providers and two Washington providers. Different states may have different consent laws and data sharing regulations. Permission Tickets can filter by jurisdiction, allowing access to only Oregon records or only Washington records, which is relevant for cross-state data exchange scenarios.

### Security Labels and Data Segmentation (Star Feature)
The headline capability this patient demonstrates is **security label–based data segmentation interacting with Permission Tickets**. The three-tier sensitivity model (general / mental health / SUD-Part 2) is concretely instantiated across five real provider sites. A downstream system receiving a Permission Ticket for Sarah's data must be able to:
1. Respect 42 CFR Part 2 consent requirements on Bridgetown Recovery Center data
2. Apply mental health sensitivity labels to psychiatric provider data
3. Handle "mixed" records at PCP sites where the problem list references sensitive diagnoses but the visit notes are general medical
4. Correctly segment the medication list by sensitivity category

### Patient Access Pattern (UC1)
Sarah is the data subject requesting her own records. She uses a patient-facing SMART app. Access tokens are bound to her identity via key binding (cnf claim). Subject resolution occurs via demographic matching (name: Sarah Elizabeth Mitchell, DOB: 1993-11-14, identifiers from each site). The demo can show Sarah viewing her complete record across all five sites, then demonstrate how a Permission Ticket can constrain what a third-party app sees — for example, Sarah might grant a new provider access to her general medical and mental health records but withhold her SUD treatment records from Bridgetown.
