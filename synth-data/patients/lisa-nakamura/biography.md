# Lisa Nakamura — Synthetic Patient Biography

## Patient Overview

**Name:** Lisa Nakamura  
**Age:** 36 (DOB: 1989-08-14)  
**Gender:** Female  
**Race/Ethnicity:** Japanese-American (race: Asian; ethnicity: Not Hispanic or Latino)  
**Preferred Language:** English  
**Marital Status:** Married — husband Ryan Cooper, age 38, high school English teacher  
**Home:** Denver, Colorado  
**Occupation:** Architectural project manager  
**Insurance:** UnitedHealthcare employer-sponsored PPO plan (through her architecture firm)

Lisa is a physically active, health-conscious woman who has been in excellent general health throughout her adult life. She practices yoga three to four times a week, hikes in the Front Range on weekends, and has never smoked. She was a light social drinker — a glass of wine with dinner on occasion — but stopped entirely when she and Ryan began trying to conceive in early 2024. She has no significant past medical history, no chronic conditions, no prior surgeries, and no known drug allergies. Her only regular medication before the fertility journey was a daily prenatal vitamin she started in January 2024.

## Clinical Arc

Lisa established care with Rocky Mountain Women's Health in 2022 after she and Ryan moved to Denver from Portland, Oregon. Her initial visits were routine — annual well-woman exams, Pap smears, HPV co-testing, and contraception management. She had been on a combined oral contraceptive pill (norgestimate-ethinyl estradiol) since her mid-twenties and tolerated it without issue. Her baseline labs were unremarkable: TSH 1.8 mIU/L, CBC normal, metabolic panel normal. She received her routine immunizations — influenza annually, Tdap booster in 2022, and COVID-19 boosters as recommended.

In January 2024, Lisa discontinued her oral contraceptive to begin trying to conceive. She and Ryan had been married for four years and felt ready to start a family. Her OB/GYN, Dr. Megan Forsythe, counseled her on preconception health, confirmed her rubella immunity, started her on prenatal vitamins with folate, and encouraged her to track her cycles. Lisa's cycles regulated within two months of stopping the pill, and she conceived in April 2024.

### First Pregnancy Loss — June 2024

Lisa's first positive pregnancy test came in early May 2024, confirmed by a serum beta-hCG of 1,240 mIU/mL at approximately 4 weeks and 5 days gestation. She was elated. A follow-up beta-hCG two days later showed appropriate doubling (2,680 mIU/mL). However, at approximately 6 weeks she developed cramping and vaginal bleeding. An urgent transvaginal ultrasound showed a gestational sac measuring consistent with 5 weeks and 6 days but no yolk sac or fetal pole — findings concerning for early pregnancy failure. Serial beta-hCG levels plateaued and then declined. Dr. Forsythe diagnosed a spontaneous abortion (missed miscarriage) and recommended expectant management. Lisa passed the products of conception at home over the following week. A follow-up beta-hCG confirmed complete resolution.

Dr. Forsythe was compassionate but reassuring. She explained that 15–20% of recognized pregnancies end in first-trimester loss, that a single miscarriage does not predict future outcomes, and that most couples go on to have successful pregnancies. She recommended waiting one full menstrual cycle before trying again and offered a follow-up appointment to check in. Lisa grieved but remained hopeful.

### Second Pregnancy Loss — November 2024

Lisa conceived again in September 2024. She presented at approximately 5 weeks for early confirmation — her anxiety was understandable — and initial beta-hCG levels rose appropriately. A 6-week ultrasound showed a gestational sac with a yolk sac, but the fetal pole was small for dates and no cardiac activity was detected. Dr. Forsythe advised cautious optimism and scheduled a repeat scan in one week. The 7-week ultrasound confirmed embryonic demise — the fetal pole had not grown, and no heartbeat was present. Lisa chose expectant management again, and the miscarriage completed spontaneously.

Now with two consecutive losses, Dr. Forsythe discussed the threshold for recurrent pregnancy loss workup. While classically defined as three or more losses, many specialists now initiate evaluation after two. Given Lisa's age (35 at the time) and her distress, Dr. Forsythe ordered a basic RPL panel:

- **Parental karyotyping:** Both Lisa and Ryan had normal karyotypes (Lisa 46,XX; Ryan 46,XY). No balanced translocations detected.
- **Thyroid function:** TSH 2.1 mIU/L (normal).
- **Hemoglobin A1c:** 5.2% (normal, no occult diabetes).
- **Basic coagulation panel:** PT, PTT, and fibrinogen all within normal limits.
- **Antinuclear antibody (ANA):** Negative.

All results were reassuring. Dr. Forsythe explained that in the majority of recurrent early losses, no identifiable cause is found, and the prognosis remains favorable. She encouraged Lisa to try again when she felt ready.

### Third Pregnancy Loss — March 2025

Lisa conceived a third time in January 2025. She was anxious from the start, and Dr. Forsythe saw her early and often. A 6-week transvaginal ultrasound was, for the first time, encouraging — it showed a fetal pole with visible cardiac activity, heart rate 112 bpm. Lisa and Ryan felt cautious relief. She was started on progesterone supplementation (vaginal micronized progesterone 200 mg nightly) as a precaution, though evidence for its benefit in her situation was limited.

At 8 weeks, the follow-up ultrasound was devastating: the fetal pole measured 7 weeks and 1 day with absent cardiac activity. The pregnancy had ended sometime in the preceding two weeks. This time, Dr. Forsythe recommended a dilation and curettage (D&C) procedure rather than expectant management, both to expedite physical recovery and — critically — to obtain products of conception for genetic analysis.

The D&C was performed at Rocky Mountain Women's Health's affiliated ambulatory surgery center. Histopathology confirmed products of conception. Cytogenetic analysis of the tissue returned a **normal female karyotype (46,XX)**, meaning the embryo itself was chromosomally normal. This was a pivotal finding: it eliminated the most common cause of sporadic early miscarriage (embryonic aneuploidy) and shifted suspicion toward a maternal factor.

### APS Workup — April 2025

Dr. Forsythe's clinical reasoning narrowed. Three early losses — including one with a chromosomally normal conceptus — in a woman with no anatomical, endocrine, or cytogenetic explanation raised suspicion for **antiphospholipid syndrome (APS)**. She ordered the APS antibody panel:

- **Anticardiolipin IgG:** 48 GPL units (positive: >40 GPL). **Elevated.**
- **Anticardiolipin IgM:** 8 MPL units (negative: <20 MPL). Normal.
- **Lupus anticoagulant (LA):** Weakly positive on dilute Russell viper venom time (dRVVT) with confirmatory mixing study.
- **Anti-beta-2 glycoprotein I IgG:** 14 units (borderline; reference <20).
- **Anti-beta-2 glycoprotein I IgM:** 6 units (negative).

The combination of elevated anticardiolipin IgG and weakly positive lupus anticoagulant was clinically significant. However, the revised Sapporo (Sydney) classification criteria for APS require that positive antiphospholipid antibodies be confirmed on **repeat testing at least 12 weeks after the initial positive result** to rule out transient positivity. This means definitive diagnosis cannot be made until approximately July 2025.

Dr. Forsythe counseled Lisa and Ryan that the findings were concerning and that APS, if confirmed, is a treatable cause of recurrent pregnancy loss. The standard treatment — low-dose aspirin plus prophylactic-dose heparin during pregnancy — significantly improves live birth rates (from ~10% untreated to ~70% treated in APS patients). But before planning another pregnancy, Lisa needed formal evaluation by specialists.

### Specialist Referrals — April–May 2025

Dr. Forsythe made two referrals:

1. **Colorado Maternal-Fetal Medicine** (Dr. Sarah Whitfield, MFM specialist) — for high-risk pregnancy planning. If APS is confirmed, any future pregnancy will be high-risk and will require close surveillance, serial ultrasounds, fetal growth monitoring, and coordinated medication management.

2. **UC Health Hematology** (Dr. James Okafor, hematologist) — for formal APS diagnostic confirmation, assessment of thrombotic risk beyond pregnancy, and design of an anticoagulation strategy for Lisa's next pregnancy and potentially longer-term.

Both referrals were accompanied by formal ServiceRequest resources directing the specialists to review Lisa's pregnancy loss history, RPL workup results, APS antibody panel, and products-of-conception genetic analysis.

### MFM Consultation — May 2025

Lisa's initial consultation with Dr. Whitfield at Colorado Maternal-Fetal Medicine was thorough. Dr. Whitfield reviewed the complete OB/GYN record from Rocky Mountain Women's Health and discussed the implications of a potential APS diagnosis. She outlined a provisional pregnancy management plan:

- Low-dose aspirin (81 mg daily) starting preconception
- Prophylactic-dose low-molecular-weight heparin (enoxaparin 40 mg subcutaneous daily) to begin upon positive pregnancy test
- First-trimester ultrasound surveillance every 2 weeks
- Serial growth ultrasounds starting at 24 weeks
- Fetal Doppler assessment if any growth concerns arise
- Planned delivery at 37–39 weeks depending on clinical course
- Delivery at a facility with a Level III NICU

Dr. Whitfield emphasized that this plan was contingent on confirmatory APS testing and coordination with hematology. A follow-up appointment was scheduled for after the confirmatory results.

### Hematology Consultation — May 2025

Dr. Okafor at UC Health Hematology saw Lisa the following week. He reviewed the same OB/GYN records and performed an independent clinical assessment. He confirmed that the antibody pattern — elevated anticardiolipin IgG plus weakly positive lupus anticoagulant — was consistent with possible APS, but he noted several nuances:

- The lupus anticoagulant was only weakly positive, which carries a lower (but still meaningful) risk profile
- Anti-beta-2 glycoprotein I was in the borderline range — worth repeating
- Lisa had no personal history of venous or arterial thrombosis, making this "obstetric APS" rather than "thrombotic APS" — a distinction that affects long-term management

He ordered:
- Repeat anticardiolipin antibodies (IgG and IgM) at 12 weeks
- Repeat lupus anticoagulant at 12 weeks
- Repeat anti-beta-2 glycoprotein I (IgG and IgM) at 12 weeks
- Complete thrombophilia panel (Factor V Leiden, prothrombin gene mutation, protein C, protein S, antithrombin) to rule out concurrent inherited thrombophilia

He scheduled a follow-up for July 2025 to review confirmatory results and finalize recommendations. If APS is confirmed, he would co-manage Lisa's anticoagulation during pregnancy in coordination with Dr. Whitfield.

### Current Status — Mid-2025

Lisa is in a holding pattern. She and Ryan are emotionally exhausted but hopeful that the suspected APS diagnosis explains their losses and that treatment will make a difference. Lisa continues her prenatal vitamin, exercises to manage stress, and sees a therapist (through her EAP) for grief and anxiety related to the losses. She is awaiting the 12-week confirmatory APS testing in July 2025 and has follow-up appointments scheduled with both Dr. Whitfield and Dr. Okafor afterward.

## Demo Relevance

### Use Case Exercised: UC7 — Provider Consult / Multi-Specialist

Lisa's scenario demonstrates a **provider-to-provider consultation** where a referring OB/GYN shares clinical data with two receiving specialists (MFM and hematology) through Permission Tickets. This is the canonical UC7 pattern from the SMART Permission Tickets specification.

### Permission Ticket Constraint Dimensions

**1. Scopes (Resource-Type Constraints)**

The two Permission Tickets issued by Rocky Mountain Women's Health constrain access to specific FHIR resource types relevant to the consultation:

- **Condition** — recurrent pregnancy loss, suspected APS
- **Observation** — APS antibody levels (anticardiolipin IgG 48 GPL, lupus anticoagulant positive), prior RPL workup labs (TSH, HbA1c, coagulation panel, karyotypes), pregnancy ultrasound findings, serial beta-hCG values
- **DiagnosticReport** — ultrasound reports, cytogenetic analysis of POC (46,XX), APS antibody panel reports
- **Procedure** — D&C procedure
- **ServiceRequest** — the originating referral from Dr. Forsythe
- **MedicationRequest** — progesterone supplementation, prenatal vitamins
- **DocumentReference** — progress notes from pregnancy loss encounters

Crucially, the Permission Tickets should **not** grant access to Lisa's behavioral health records at Mindful Pathways Counseling or to data unrelated to the reproductive health consultation (e.g., routine preventive screenings from unrelated well-woman visits if those were at a separate site).

**2. Organizations (Requester and Data Holder)**

- **Data Holder:** Rocky Mountain Women's Health (NPI: 1346782510) — holds the authoritative clinical record
- **Requester 1:** Colorado Maternal-Fetal Medicine (NPI: 1782345691) — Dr. Sarah Whitfield requesting for MFM consult
- **Requester 2:** UC Health Hematology (NPI: 1623478905) — Dr. James Okafor requesting for APS evaluation

This demonstrates **two separate Permission Tickets** from the same data holder to two different requesting organizations for the same patient, each with a distinct clinical reason. The tickets share the same resource-type scope but serve different specialists with different clinical questions.

**3. Practitioner-Level Requesters**

Each ticket identifies the requesting practitioner by NPI:
- Dr. Sarah Whitfield (NPI: 1467823159) — MFM
- Dr. James Okafor (NPI: 1234567893) — Hematology

This demonstrates subject resolution by reference (Lisa is already known to the data holder) and requester identification at both the organizational and individual practitioner level.

**4. Reason and Clinical Context**

Both Permission Tickets include:
- **Reason:** Recurrent pregnancy loss (SNOMED CT: 156073005 or relevant code) and suspected antiphospholipid syndrome (SNOMED CT: 726078006)
- **Supporting Reference:** ServiceRequest from Dr. Forsythe documenting the referral

**5. Temporal Dimension**

The APS diagnostic journey has a built-in temporal arc:
- Initial positive antibodies: April 2025
- Confirmatory testing required: ≥12 weeks later (July 2025)
- The Permission Tickets need to remain valid across this confirmatory window
- New data (repeat antibody results) will be generated at the hematology site and may need to flow back to the OB/GYN

**6. Sensitivity and Security Labels**

Lisa's data involves reproductive health, which carries inherent sensitivity considerations:
- Pregnancy loss history is sensitive personal health information
- APS diagnosis has implications beyond obstetrics (insurance, life planning)
- The existence of behavioral health records (therapy) at a separate site highlights why scope constraints prevent over-sharing
- Colorado state law may impose additional protections on reproductive health data

### What Makes This Scenario Compelling

Lisa's story is medically realistic and emotionally resonant. The diagnostic journey — from a "normal" first miscarriage through the gradual accumulation of evidence pointing to APS — mirrors real clinical reasoning. The three-site, single-state topology is simple enough to implement cleanly while exercising the core Permission Ticket mechanics: scoped access, organizational trust chains, practitioner-level authorization, and the temporal dynamics of an evolving diagnostic workup. The two parallel specialist consultations from the same referring provider create a natural demonstration of how the same UC7 pattern can be instantiated multiple times for different clinical purposes.
