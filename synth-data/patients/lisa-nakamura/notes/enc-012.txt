ASSESSMENT:
1. Suspected antiphospholipid syndrome, obstetric subtype — The combination of elevated anticardiolipin IgG (48 GPL) and weakly positive lupus anticoagulant in the setting of three recurrent early pregnancy losses (including one with chromosomally normal POC) is consistent with possible APS. However, I note the following nuances:
   a. The lupus anticoagulant is only weakly positive, conferring a lower but still clinically meaningful thrombotic and obstetric risk compared to strongly positive results.
   b. Anti-beta-2 glycoprotein I IgG is in the borderline range at 14 units. This antibody has the strongest correlation with thrombotic risk in APS and warrants repeat testing.
   c. The patient has no personal history of venous or arterial thrombosis. This represents "obstetric APS" rather than "thrombotic APS," a distinction that significantly affects long-term management.
   d. Per the revised Sapporo (Sydney) classification criteria, positive antiphospholipid antibodies must persist on repeat testing at least 12 weeks after the initial positive result to exclude transient positivity from infection or inflammatory states.

2. Recurrent pregnancy loss — Three consecutive early losses with no identified anatomical, endocrine, or cytogenetic etiology. APS is the leading suspected cause.
